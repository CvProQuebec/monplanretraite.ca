# RRSP vs TFSA: Which to Choose at 55 and Over?

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 11 minutes*

**At 55, you're entering the most decisive period of your financial planning. You're likely earning your highest salary, and you have 10 to 16 years before mandatory RRIF withdrawals kick in. This is a golden window — but it closes. Here's how to make the most of it.**

---

## 📋 Quick Reference: RRSP vs TFSA at a Glance

| Feature | RRSP | TFSA |
|---------|------|------|
| **Contributions** | Tax-deductible | No deduction |
| **Growth** | Tax-sheltered | Tax-sheltered |
| **Withdrawals** | 100% taxable | Completely tax-free |
| **Mandatory withdrawals** | Yes — starting at age 72 (RRIF) | Never |
| **Impact on OAS / GIS** | Yes — withdrawals count as income | No — TFSA withdrawals don't count |
| **Age limit** | Convert to RRIF by age 71 | No age limit |
| **2026 limits** | 18% of earned income, max $33,810 | $7,000/year (cumulative: $109,000) |

---

## 🔍 Why the Strategy Changes at 55

Before 55, the general rule is straightforward: if your marginal tax rate is high, prioritize the RRSP. After 55, the dynamics become more complex.

**What changes at 55:**
- You're potentially at your peak career income → maximum RRSP tax savings
- You have only 10 to 16 years before mandatory RRIF withdrawals (age 72)
- Old Age Security (OAS) is on the horizon — RRIF income could trigger a clawback
- Liquidity needs increase (health, early retirement, major projects)

The tension is between two forces: **saving taxes now with the RRSP** versus **building tax-free withdrawals with the TFSA**.

---

## 💼 The Most Overlooked Advantage: Employer Matching

If your employer offers a group plan with matching contributions, this is **the most profitable investment that exists** — and there's no TFSA equivalent.

**Example: Julie, age 56**
- Salary: $90,000/year
- Employer: matches 50% up to 5% of salary → $2,250/year if Julie contributes $4,500
- Instant return: **50%** on that $4,500 — before any investment return

**Golden rule:** Before choosing between RRSP and TFSA, always contribute enough to the employer plan to get the maximum matching contribution. It's a guaranteed return you can't afford to ignore.

## ⏳ The Golden Window: The RRSP Meltdown Strategy

The least-known — and most powerful — concept for the 55+ age group is the **RRSP meltdown** strategy. It means gradually withdrawing from your RRSP *before* you're forced to, while your taxable income still gives you room.

### Why Start the Meltdown Now?

At age 72, mandatory RRIF withdrawals will stack on top of your CPP/QPP, OAS, and other income. If your RRSP is still very large at 72, you'll have **no choice** about timing or amount — and your marginal rate could be high.

By contrast, if you withdraw gradually from age 58–65, you control the pace and the tax rate.

### How It Works

1. **Calculate your available room** in your current tax bracket (fixed income − next bracket threshold)
2. **Withdraw the optimal RRSP amount** to fill that bracket without exceeding it
3. **Move the surplus into your TFSA** — it then grows tax-sheltered permanently
4. **Defer CPP/QPP and OAS** to maximize the bracket room available

### Example: Sylvie, Age 60, Employer Pension of $42,000/Year

**Without RRSP meltdown:**
- At 72, RRSP = $500,000 → mandatory minimum: ~$27,000/year
- Total income: $42,000 (pension) + $27,000 (RRIF) + $15,000 (CPP) + $8,500 (OAS) = **$92,500**
- OAS clawback risk (2026 threshold: $95,323) — dangerously close

**With RRSP meltdown from age 60 to 70:**
- Annual withdrawal: $15,000/year from RRSP (within the lowest bracket)
- RRSP reduced to ~$350,000 at 72 → mandatory minimum: ~$19,000/year
- Total income at 72: $42,000 + $19,000 + $15,000 + $8,500 = **$84,500** — well under the OAS threshold
- **Lifetime savings: $40,000 to $100,000**

## 🚨 The RRIF Trap: Mandatory Withdrawals

**At age 72, RRIF withdrawals become mandatory** — and the percentage increases every year:

| Age | Minimum % of RRIF balance | Example on $300,000 |
|-----|--------------------------|----------------------|
| 72 | 5.40% | $16,200/year |
| 75 | 5.82% | $17,460/year |
| 80 | 6.82% | $20,460/year |
| 85 | 8.51% | $25,530/year |
| 90 | 11.92% | $35,760/year |

These withdrawals are added to all your other income. If your total income exceeds **$95,323 in 2026**, you lose 15 cents of OAS for every dollar above the threshold.

**Little-known tip — spouse's age:**
If your spouse is younger, you can choose their age to calculate your minimum RRIF withdrawals — reducing the mandatory amounts. This choice is **permanent once minimum withdrawals begin**: you cannot change it later. Analyze this carefully before making your first withdrawal.

## 🏆 The Advantages of TFSA After 55

### 1. Complete Flexibility for the Unexpected
From age 55, unexpected events multiply: health issues, early retirement, helping children, travel, renovations. With a TFSA, you can withdraw any amount at any time, **without penalty and without tax impact**.

### 2. Full OAS and GIS Protection
TFSA withdrawals don't count in your taxable income. In 2026, if your income stays under **$95,323** thanks to TFSA withdrawals instead of RRIF withdrawals, your OAS is untouched. Every TFSA dollar = 15 cents of OAS protected.

### 3. No Mandatory Withdrawals — Ever
Your TFSA has no age limit and no minimum withdrawal will ever be imposed. You maintain complete control over your capital for life.

### 4. Contribution Room Recovers Automatically
Amounts you withdraw from the TFSA are added back to your contribution room the following January. You can "recycle" your TFSA indefinitely — for example, withdrawing from RRSP/RRIF and redepositing into the TFSA.

### 5. Tax-Free Transfer to Spouse
At your death, a spouse designated as "successor holder" inherits your TFSA **tax-free and without using their own contribution room**. A RRIF, by contrast, is fully taxable in the year of death if both spouses are deceased.

### 6. Substantial Cumulative Room in 2026
If you're 55 and were eligible since 2009, your available cumulative room is **$109,000** — a significant tax shelter.

## ✅ When Is the RRSP Still Advantageous After 55?

The RRSP isn't obsolete at 55. It remains very relevant in these situations:

### Situation A: Very High Income Now, Modest Retirement Planned
- Current marginal rate: 45%+
- Expected rate in retirement: 25–30%
- Net advantage per dollar contributed: **15 to 20 cents saved**

### Situation B: Employer with Matching Contributions
As noted, this is automatic: always contribute up to the matching limit first.

### Situation C: No Employer Pension
If you'll only have CPP/QPP and OAS in retirement (low income), your retirement tax rate will likely be lower than today → the RRSP makes sense.

### Situation D: Spouse with Low or No Income
The **spousal RRSP** lets the higher earner claim the deduction while the lower-income spouse owns and later withdraws the funds — at their lower tax rate. Respect the 3-year attribution rule before withdrawing.

### Situation E: You're Planning the RRSP Meltdown
If you plan to gradually withdraw your RRSP between ages 60 and 71 at lower tax rates, contributing now (high rate) and withdrawing later (low rate) is exactly what the RRSP was designed for.

## 💡 The Micro-RRIF at 65: Unlocking Two Credits

Here's an underused strategy that's particularly powerful for the 55+ group:

At age 65 (not 72), you can convert a **small portion** of your RRSP to a RRIF and start withdrawing about $12,000/year.

**Why?** RRIF withdrawals after age 65 qualify as "eligible pension income" — which unlocks the **pension income tax credit** (up to $2,000 of eligible income, worth approximately **$600** in combined federal/provincial tax savings).

**The double benefit with income splitting:**
1. Convert $20,000–$30,000 of RRSP to a RRIF at age 65
2. Withdraw ~$12,000/year
3. Split 50% with your spouse → each reports $6,000
4. **Both claim the pension income credit**
5. Result: two credits instead of one → savings doubled (~$1,200 instead of $600)

## 🔒 Creditor Protection: An Often Forgotten Factor

**RRSP/RRIF:** Protected from creditors under the *Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act*. If you face financial difficulty or are a self-employed person with liability exposure, your RRSP/RRIF funds are generally sheltered.

**TFSA:** Not protected in most provinces. In bankruptcy, your TFSA could be seized.

If you're a business owner, professional with litigation risk, or self-employed, this factor can weigh in favor of the RRSP.

## 👤 Typical Profiles: Which One Are You?

### Profile 1 — Claire, Age 56, Income $105,000, Retirement at 63

**Situation:** Current marginal rate ~48%. Expects $45,000 employer pension at 63, then CPP and OAS.

**Recommendation:**
- Contribute to RRSP up to the limit ($33,810 in 2026) → ~$16,000 tax savings/year
- Maximize TFSA ($7,000/year)
- At 63, begin RRSP meltdown gradually in lower brackets (before CPP and OAS)
- Defer CPP/QPP until 70 to free up bracket room

### Profile 2 — Robert, Age 57, Income $75,000, No Employer Pension

**Situation:** Marginal rate ~38%. Retirement at 65, only CPP/QPP and OAS expected.

**Recommendation:**
- $7,000/year in TFSA (top priority for flexibility)
- $8,000/year in RRSP (tax savings ~$3,000)
- At 65, micro-RRIF to unlock the pension income tax credit
- Use TFSA for discretionary expenses in retirement without affecting OAS

**Projected result at 67:**
- TFSA: ~$100,000 available tax-free
- RRSP: ~$115,000 as a source of regular retirement income

### Profile 3 — Sophie, Age 59, Modest Income $45,000, Existing RRSP of $180,000

**Situation:** RRSP already significant relative to income. Concerned about high mandatory withdrawals at 72.

**Recommendation:**
- Stop RRSP contributions (limited tax advantage at her rate)
- Maximize TFSA each year
- Begin RRSP meltdown now: withdraw ~$10,000/year from RRSP, pay tax at low rate, transfer to TFSA
- Protect OAS for life

## ❌ The 5 Mistakes to Avoid After 55

### Mistake 1: Putting Everything in RRSP Without Planning Withdrawals
Building a massive RRSP without an exit strategy creates a tax problem at 72. Plan the meltdown now.

### Mistake 2: Treating the TFSA as an Emergency Fund Only
The TFSA is your most powerful tax tool in retirement. Invest your long-term growth assets there, not just cash.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Employer Matching
If your employer offers matching and you're not contributing to the maximum match, you're leaving free money on the table.

### Mistake 4: Taking CPP/QPP and OAS Too Early
Taking CPP at 60 and OAS at 65 fills your tax brackets — exactly the room you needed for RRSP meltdown withdrawals. These two strategies work against each other.

### Mistake 5: Not Checking Your Cumulative TFSA Room
Tens of thousands of Canadians don't realize they have $109,000 of cumulative TFSA room available. Check on CRA My Account.

## ❓ Frequently Asked Questions

### "I already have a lot in my RRSP — what should I do?"

Start the RRSP meltdown now, progressively. Withdraw what your tax bracket allows and transfer to TFSA. Don't let your RRSP keep growing until age 72 — mandatory withdrawals could trigger OAS clawbacks.

### "My advisor says to keep contributing to RRSP. Who's right?"

Your advisor knows your complete situation. But ask them: Have they simulated the impact of mandatory RRIF withdrawals at 72 on your OAS? Have they compared your tax rate today vs. in retirement? Have they quantified the RRSP meltdown option?

### "Can I have both?"

Yes — it's often the best strategy. Contribute first to the employer matching limit, then maximize the TFSA, then the RRSP based on your capacity and tax situation.

### "When should I convert my RRSP to a RRIF?"

You *must* convert before the end of the year you turn 71. But you *can* start withdrawals (via a partial RRIF) as early as age 65 to access the pension income tax credit. If you're planning an RRSP meltdown, it's often advantageous to start before age 65.

## 💡 The Bottom Line

At 55 and over, the TFSA often becomes more advantageous because it protects OAS and provides complete flexibility. But the RRSP retains real advantages — especially if you have high income today and plan a gradual meltdown.

**The optimal strategy for most people:**
1. Contribute to the maximum employer matching (if available)
2. Maximize the TFSA ($7,000/year, cumulative $109,000 in 2026)
3. Contribute to RRSP if your marginal rate is high AND you plan a gradual meltdown
4. Plan your decumulation now — wait until 71 and it will be too late
