# How to Save on Your Retirement Taxes

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 13 minutes*

**Great news: in retirement, you have more control over your tax bill than at any other point in your life. When you were working, tax was withheld at source — you had no choice. Now, *you* decide how much to withdraw, from which source, and when. That control is worth tens of thousands of dollars if you use it wisely.**

---

## 💡 **The Golden Rule: Source Matters as Much as Amount**

Here's a reality most retirees don't know:

**$60,000 in eligible Canadian dividends** → tax close to **$0**
**$60,000 in interest income** → tax of about **$18,000**

Same amount, same person. The difference? The *source* of the income.

In retirement, you can often choose where your income comes from — and that choice is one of the most powerful tax decisions you can make.

---

## 📊 **The 4 Types of Retirement Income and How They're Taxed**

| Income Type | Tax Treatment | Examples |
|------------|---------------|---------|
| **Fully taxable** | 100% | RRIF, RRSP, CPP, OAS, employer pensions, interest income, rental income |
| **Partially taxable** | 50% of gain | Capital gains (stocks, real estate) |
| **Tax-advantaged** | Reduced rate (dividend tax credit) | Eligible Canadian dividends (RBC, TD, Bell, etc.) |
| **Completely tax-free** | 0% | TFSA withdrawals, inheritance received, proceeds from selling your principal residence |

**Tax-priority order for withdrawals:**
1. TFSA first (0% tax, doesn't count as income)
2. Non-registered accounts with capital gains or dividends (favourable treatment)
3. RRIF/RRSP strategically (see the withdrawal order article for the full framework)

## 🏆 **Strategy #1: The TFSA — Your #1 Control Tool**

### Why the TFSA Is Irreplaceable in Retirement

The TFSA isn't just a savings account — it's your **tax-free income tap**. Every dollar withdrawn from the TFSA:
- Does **not** appear in your taxable income
- Does **not** trigger OAS clawbacks
- Does **not** reduce your GIS or tax credits
- Does **not** count against provincial income-tested programs

In 2026, if you turned 18 and were a Canadian resident in 2009, your cumulative contribution room is **$109,000**. If you've never contributed, that's a powerful untapped tool.

### The RRIF Surplus → TFSA Strategy

When you must take your mandatory RRIF withdrawal but don't need all of it immediately:
1. Withdraw your mandatory minimum from the RRIF (taxable, but unavoidable)
2. Use what you need for living expenses
3. **Deposit the surplus into your TFSA** — it grows tax-free from that point forward

**Example: Jacques, age 72**
- RRIF balance: $50,000 → mandatory minimum: $2,700
- Actual monthly needs: $1,500
- Surplus: $1,200 → deposited to TFSA
- Impact: that money now grows completely sheltered from future tax

### Invest in Your Family's TFSAs

If you've used all your room, you can give money to your spouse or adult children to contribute to their own TFSAs. There are no tax consequences for you, and the money grows tax-free in their accounts.

## 🏠 **Strategy #2: Investment Location**

*Where* you hold your investments can be just as important as *what* you hold. Each income type has an ideal account:

| Income Type | Ideal Account | Why |
|-------------|---------------|-----|
| **Interest income** (GICs, bonds) | RRSP/RRIF | 100% taxable → better sheltered |
| **Eligible Canadian dividends** | Non-registered account | Dividend tax credit offers favourable treatment |
| **Growth / capital gains** | TFSA first | Growth is completely tax-free |
| **Foreign dividend stocks** | RRSP/RRIF | Foreign withholding tax often recoverable via treaty |

**Annual savings example**: A $400,000 portfolio with the right assets in the right accounts can generate an extra **$5,000 to $15,000** in annual tax savings — without changing the investments themselves.

## 👫 **Strategy #3: Income Splitting**

Canada's tax system is progressive: the more you earn, the higher your marginal rate. If two spouses have very unequal incomes, household tax optimization can generate substantial savings.

### A) Pension Income Splitting (Age 65+)

Starting at 65, you can transfer **up to 50%** of your eligible pension income to your spouse via form T1032:
- RRIF withdrawals ✓
- Defined benefit employer pensions ✓
- Life annuities ✓

**Example: Andrew ($90,000) and Louise ($30,000)**
- Without splitting: total household tax ≈ $18,500/year
- With 50% splitting: total household tax ≈ $13,800/year
- **Annual savings: $4,700 → $70,500 over 15 years**

### B) The "Micro-RRIF at 65" Trick — Unlocking Two Credits

Even if you don't need to touch your RRSP yet, consider converting a small portion to a RRIF at age 65.

**Why?** RRIF withdrawals after 65 qualify as "eligible pension income," which unlocks the **pension income tax credit** — up to $2,000 of eligible income, worth about **$600 in combined federal/provincial tax savings**.

**The strategy:**
1. Convert $20,000–$30,000 of RRSP to a RRIF at age 65
2. Withdraw ~$12,000/year from the RRIF
3. Split 50% with your spouse → each reports $6,000
4. Both claim the pension income credit
5. Result: **two credits** instead of one → savings doubled

### C) CPP/QPP Pension Sharing

You and your spouse can apply to share your CPP/QPP pensions, equalizing income levels and reducing the household tax bill. This works even if contributions were uneven.

### D) Spousal RRSP Contributions (Before Age 71)

If you still have RRSP contribution room and are under age 71, contributing to a spousal RRSP lets the higher earner claim the deduction while the lower earner owns and later withdraws the funds — at their lower tax rate. Respect the 3-year attribution rule before withdrawing.

## 🚨 **Strategy #4: Avoiding the OAS Clawback**

The OAS clawback threshold is often misunderstood — and expensive for those who cross it unknowingly.

**2026 Threshold: $95,323** (↑ from $93,454 in 2025)
**Rate**: 15 cents lost for every dollar above the threshold

What **counts** as income (can trigger clawback):
- RRIF withdrawals ✓ | CPP/QPP ✓ | OAS itself ✓
- Non-registered investment income ✓ | Rental income ✓

What does **NOT count** (no clawback impact):
- TFSA withdrawals ✗ | Return of capital ✗ | Life insurance proceeds ✗

**Your 4 shields against OAS clawback:**

1. **TFSA before RRIF**: Every dollar from the TFSA avoids 15 cents of OAS clawback
2. **Income splitting**: Transferring 50% of pension income to your spouse reduces your individual income
3. **Withdrawal planning**: Take only the mandatory RRIF minimum; direct surplus to TFSA
4. **RRSP meltdown before 71**: Reducing RRIF balance in advance limits future mandatory withdrawals (see the withdrawal order article)

## 📈 **Strategy #5: Optimizing CPP/QPP and OAS Timing**

Deferring CPP/QPP and OAS generates permanent, inflation-indexed increases:

| Start Age | CPP/QPP Impact | OAS Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Age 60 | -36% (vs age 65) | N/A |
| Age 65 | Base amount | Base amount |
| Age 70 | **+42%** | **+36%** |

For someone entitled to $1,000/month CPP at 65, waiting until 70 means **$1,420/month for life**. The break-even point is typically around age 78–79.

**The synergy with the RRSP meltdown**: Deferring CPP and OAS creates the tax bracket room needed to draw down the RRSP at lower rates between ages 60 and 70. The two strategies reinforce each other (see the smart withdrawal order article for the full framework).

## 🎁 **Strategy #6: Maximize Your Special Tax Credits**

### Age Credit (Age 65+)

This federal and provincial credit can reduce your tax by **several hundred dollars per year**. It phases out gradually as income rises — another reason to keep income moderate.

### Pension Income Credit

- Applies to employer pensions and RRIF withdrawals **starting at age 65**
- Maximum: **$2,000** of eligible pension income
- Savings: approximately **$600** in combined tax
- **Both spouses can claim it** if each receives eligible pension income — see the micro-RRIF strategy above

### Other Senior Credits

- **Medical expense credit**: More generous than you think — prescription drugs, dental care, glasses, private health insurance premiums, certain accessibility renovations
- **Caregiver credit**: If you support a family member
- **Home support credit** (Quebec): Home care services, adaptations, attendant care

**Pro tip**: Keep *all* medical receipts throughout the year. Many retirees leave this credit unclaimed simply for lack of documentation.

## 💝 **Strategy #7: Charitable Giving — Donate Securities, Not Cash**

If you're philanthropically inclined, this underused strategy is particularly powerful.

**The usual method (less efficient):**
1. Sell your shares (triggers capital gains tax)
2. Pay the tax
3. Donate the reduced net amount → receipt based on the lower amount

**The optimal method — donate securities in kind:**
1. Transfer the shares directly to the charity (without selling)
2. You receive a tax receipt for the **full market value**
3. **The capital gain is exempt** — you pay zero tax on the appreciation

**Example:**
- Shares bought for $6,000, now worth $10,000 (gain: $4,000)
- Cash donation route: sell → pay ~$600 tax → donate $9,400 → receipt for $9,400
- In-kind donation: give shares → **$0 tax** → receipt for **$10,000** → combined saving of $3,000–$4,000

In a high-income year (large withdrawals, asset sales), a strategic in-kind donation can meaningfully reduce your tax bill and OAS clawback risk.

## 💰 **Concrete Example: Jean and Marie, Retired Couple**

**Situation:**
- Jean, age 70, RRIF of $200,000, fixed income $40,000/year
- Marie, age 67, fixed income $25,000/year
- No TFSA used to date — each has $109,000 in cumulative room

**Without optimization:**
- Jean withdraws $10,800 from RRIF (mandatory minimum)
- Jean's income: $50,800 → estimated tax: ~$11,000
- Marie's income: $25,000 → estimated tax: ~$3,500
- Household tax: **$14,500**

**With 2026 strategies applied:**
1. Jean withdraws RRIF minimum ($10,800) and splits 50% with Marie → each reports $5,400 pension income
2. Both claim pension income credit
3. RRIF surplus goes into Jean's TFSA
4. Marie uses her TFSA for discretionary spending
5. GICs held inside RRIF; dividend stocks in non-registered accounts

- Jean's taxable income (after splitting): ~$45,400 → tax: ~$8,200
- Marie's taxable income (after splitting): ~$30,400 → tax: ~$4,600
- Household tax: **$12,800**
- **Annual savings: $1,700 → $25,500 over 15 years**

If Marie also uses her TFSA instead of non-registered accounts for additional withdrawals, the compound savings over a full retirement could easily double.

## 🚫 **5 Costly Mistakes to Avoid**

### Mistake #1: Letting TFSA Room Sit Unused

$109,000 of unused TFSA room is an enormous tax tool left on the table. Even without fresh savings, your annual RRIF surplus can fund the TFSA every year.

### Mistake #2: Not Splitting Pension Income

Thousands of couples never file form T1032. Yet for a couple with unequal incomes, pension income splitting alone can save $3,000–$8,000 per year.

### Mistake #3: Ignoring Investment Location

Keeping GICs in a TFSA (already sheltered) while growth stocks sit in a RRIF (where they'll be taxed at 100% on withdrawal) costs thousands of dollars in avoidable tax each year.

### Mistake #4: Taking All Government Pensions Too Early

Claiming CPP at 60 and OAS at 65 without analysis can cost tens of thousands of dollars in permanently reduced lifetime income and unnecessary OAS clawbacks.

### Mistake #5: Not Reviewing Your Strategy Annually

Tax thresholds change (the OAS threshold jumped by over $4,000 in one year!), account balances shift, and life circumstances evolve. An annual review each fall — before year-end — can capture significant savings.
