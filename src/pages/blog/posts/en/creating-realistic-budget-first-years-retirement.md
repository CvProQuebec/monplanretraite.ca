# Creating a Realistic Budget for Your First Years of Retirement

*Updated March 1, 2026*

Approaching retirement and wondering how to plan your budget? You're right to ask! The first years of retirement are special: that's when you fulfill your dreams while learning to live with a new lifestyle. Here's how to create a realistic budget that lets you fully enjoy this wonderful chapter.

## Why the First Years Are Different

Experts agree: your expenses will change throughout your retirement. Being 65 is not the same as being 80!

### The Three Phases of Retirement

**Phase 1: The First Years (65–75)**
- You're in good shape and full of projects
- Your expenses are often higher
- It's time for travel and active leisure

**Phase 2: The Middle Years (75–85)**
- You slow down a bit
- Your expenses generally decrease
- You go out less and travel less

**Phase 3: The Later Years (85+)**
- Your healthcare costs may rise
- You need more help and care
- Some expenses rise again

## The 70% Rule: Your Starting Point

Experts say that in retirement, you need about **70% of your former salary** to maintain your standard of living. But good news: according to financial planner Nasser Mama, you can often live comfortably with just **50% of your former salary**!

### Why You Need Less Money

Several expenses will disappear or decrease:

**Gone are the work costs:**
- No more office clothes to buy
- No more business lunches
- No more gas for the commute
- No more downtown parking

**Less financial pressure:**
- Your mortgage may be paid off
- Your children are probably independent
- You're no longer saving for retirement

## Step 1: Calculate Your Retirement Income

Before building your budget, you need to know how much money will come in each month.

### Your Income Sources

**Government programs:**
- **Quebec Pension Plan (QPP)**: Approximately 25% to 33% of your former salary. 2026 maximum: approximately **$1,433/month** at 65 (QPP) | **$1,395/month** (CPP outside Quebec)
- **Old Age Security (OAS)**: 2026 amount: approximately **$727/month** (ages 65–74) | **$800/month** (age 75+). *Note:* clawed back at 15% if net income exceeds **$95,323** in 2026
- **Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS)**: If your income is low. 2026 maximum: approximately **$1,100/month** (single person) — tax-free

**Your personal savings:**
- **RRSP converted to RRIF**: Mandatory withdrawals based on your age
- **TFSA**: Withdraw whenever you want — no tax impact
- **Other investments**: Stocks, bonds, funds

**Other possible sources:**
- Employer pension plan
- Sale of assets (home, cottage, car)
- Part-time work

### The RRIF: Your Main Source

At 71, you must convert your RRSPs into a RRIF (Registered Retirement Income Fund). You must then withdraw a minimum amount each year:

- **At 71**: 5.28% of your RRIF value
- **At 72**: 5.40%
- **At 75**: 5.82%
- **At 80**: 6.82%

**Concrete example:**
If you have $200,000 in your RRIF at 71, you must withdraw at minimum:
$200,000 × 5.28% = $10,560 per year (about $880 per month)

> 💡 **Key strategy**: Start making gradual RRSP withdrawals between ages 60 and 70, when your income is lower. This reduces your RRIF size at 71–72 — protecting your OAS from the clawback threshold ($95,323) and saving taxes.

## Step 2: Identify Your Retirement Expenses

Now, on to expenses! This is where it gets concrete.

### Your Basic Costs (The Essentials)

**Housing:**
- Mortgage or rent
- Municipal and school taxes
- Home insurance
- Maintenance and repairs
- Heating and electricity

**Food:**
- Groceries
- Some restaurant outings
- Medications and vitamins

**Transportation:**
- Car payments or public transit
- Gas and maintenance
- Car insurance

**Health:**
- Drug insurance
- Dental care
- Eyeglasses
- Massage therapy, physiotherapy

### Your Pleasure Expenses (Lifestyle)

**In the first years, you want to enjoy life:**
- **Travel**: Often the top priority!
- **Leisure**: Golf, fishing, reading, movies
- **Outings**: Restaurants, shows, visits with children
- **Hobbies**: Courses, crafts, gardening

### How to Prioritize Your Expenses

Make a list and rank by importance:
1. What is absolutely necessary
2. What truly makes you happy
3. What would be nice to have, but not essential

## Step 3: Build Your Budget Step by Step

### Simple 6-Step Method

**Step 1: List Everything (15 minutes)**
- Take a blank sheet
- Write down all the expenses that come to mind
- Look at your account statements from recent months
- **You've done 40% of the work!**

**Step 2: The Details (20 minutes)**
Think of expenses often forgotten:
- Hairdresser, dentist
- Birthday and Christmas gifts
- Home repairs
- Insurance
- **You're now at 80%!**

**Step 3: Get Out the Calculator (30 minutes)**
- Add everything up by category
- Add 5% for unexpected expenses
- **Excellent! Your budget is 95% done**

**Step 4: Make Your Choices (1 hour)**
- Compare your income and expenses
- Cut where it matters less
- Keep what truly makes you happy

**Step 5: Test Your Budget (3 months)**
- Try to live with your budget
- Note where you go over
- Adjust as needed

**Step 6: Meet with a Professional**
- A financial planner can give excellent advice
- They can help you optimize your taxes
- They ensure your money lasts long enough

## Realistic Budget for the First Years

Here's a concrete example to inspire you:

### Retired Couple, Age 65, Combined Former Salary of $80,000

**Monthly income:**
- QPP (both): $2,000
- Old Age Security (both, ~$727/month each): $1,454
- RRIF: $1,200
- **Total: approximately $4,654/month**

**Monthly expenses:**

**Essentials ($3,000):**
- Housing (taxes, insurance, maintenance): $800
- Food: $600
- Transportation: $400
- Insurance and healthcare: $500
- Clothing and personal care: $200
- Phone and internet: $100
- Miscellaneous and unexpected: $400

**Pleasure ($1,400):**
- Outings and restaurants: $400
- Travel: $500
- Leisure and hobbies: $300
- Gifts and donations: $200

**Total: $4,400/month**
**Surplus: ~$254/month** (for unexpected expenses or treats)

## Practical Tips for Success

### Be Realistic, Not a Perfectionist

- Your first budget won't be perfect
- It's normal to need adjustments
- The important thing is to start

### Plan for Changes

**Your expenses will evolve:**
- **Ages 65–70**: More travel and leisure
- **Ages 70–80**: More stable expenses
- **Ages 80+**: More healthcare costs

### Keep a Safety Cushion

- Keep 3 to 6 months of expenses in savings
- Put this money in an easily accessible account
- Only touch this money in case of emergency

### Watch for Inflation

Inflation increases prices each year. With 3% inflation annually, what costs $100 today will cost:
- **$130 in 10 years**
- **$180 in 20 years**

Make sure your income can keep up!

## Mistakes to Avoid

### Not Planning Far Enough Ahead

Your retirement can last 30 years! Don't plan just for the first 5 years.

### Underestimating Healthcare Costs

As you age, you'll likely need:
- More medications
- Home care
- Special equipment
- Possibly a seniors' residence

### Being Too Conservative or Too Spendthrift

- Don't live like a pauper if you have the means
- But don't splurge everything in the early years

### Forgetting About Taxes

Your RRIF withdrawals are taxable. If you withdraw $1,000, you may only have $650 to $750 after taxes.

## Strategies to Optimize Your Budget

### Reduce Expenses Smartly

**Sell what you no longer use:**
- Second car
- A cottage you rarely use
- Sports equipment

**Move if it makes sense:**
- Smaller home = lower costs
- Apartment = more freedom
- Another region = lower cost of living

### Increase Your Income

**Part-time work:**
- Consult your former employer
- Teach or mentor
- Leverage your talents (cooking, handiwork)

**Optimize your investments:**
- Meet with an advisor
- Diversify your investments
- Take advantage of tax benefits

## The Importance of Regular Review

### Review Your Budget Every Year

- Your needs change
- Prices rise
- Your income may change

### Signs It's Time to Adjust

- You often go over your budget
- You always have money left over
- Your health changes
- You have new projects

## In Summary: Your Tools for Success

### The Recipe for Success

1. **Know your income**: QPP/CPP, OAS, RRIF, other sources
2. **List your expenses**: essentials and pleasures
3. **Use the right tools**: budgets, calculators
4. **Plan for changes**: your needs will evolve
5. **Keep a cushion**: for the unexpected
6. **Review regularly**: at least once a year

### Remember

- **50% to 70%** of your former salary is often enough
- **The first years** are often the most expensive
- **A perfect budget doesn't exist** — the important thing is to have one
- **You deserve to enjoy** your retirement!

## Conclusion: Your Retirement, Your Budget, Your Freedom

Creating a budget for your first years of retirement isn't about deprivation. It's about giving yourself the freedom to enjoy your dreams without worrying about money.

Start simple: a sheet, a pencil, and your account statements. In a few hours, you'll have a much clearer picture of your financial situation.

And remember: you've worked your whole life to reach this moment. You deserve a great retirement! With a good budget, you can achieve your projects while keeping your peace of mind.

Don't hesitate to ask for professional help if you feel overwhelmed. Your retirement is too important to leave to chance. Happy budgeting and happy retirement!
