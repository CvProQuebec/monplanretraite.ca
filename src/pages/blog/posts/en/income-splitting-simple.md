# Income Splitting Explained Simply

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 10 minutes*

**Income splitting is one of the most powerful tax strategies available to Canadian couples in retirement. Yet thousands of couples never fill out form T1032 — leaving $3,000 to $10,000 on the table every year. Here's how to avoid that mistake.**

---

## 💡 Why It Works: The Logic of Tax Brackets

In Canada, income tax works through **progressive brackets**: the more you earn, the higher the rate on your last dollars. Two moderate incomes are fiscally more efficient than one high income.

**The golden rule:** Two lower incomes are better than one high income!

**Concrete example — Andrew ($90,000) and Louise ($30,000):**

| Scenario | Andrew's Tax | Louise's Tax | Couple's Total |
|----------|-------------|-------------|----------------|
| Without splitting | ~$14,000 | ~$4,500 | **$18,500** |
| With optimal splitting (~$60,000 each) | ~$9,000 | ~$4,800 | **$13,800** |
| **Annual savings** | | | **$4,700** |

**Over 15 years of retirement: $70,500 in savings** — just by filling out a form each year.

## 📋 The 4 Main Income Splitting Strategies

### Strategy 1 — Pension Income Splitting (Form T1032)

**The most common and accessible method — and it costs nothing.**

**Eligible income for splitting at age 65 and over:**
- RRIF withdrawals ✓
- Defined benefit employer pensions ✓
- Life annuities from registered plans ✓

**Income NOT eligible (important to know):**
- CPP/QPP ✗ (separate strategy — see below)
- Interest income or dividends ✗
- OAS ✗

**Limit:** Up to **50% of eligible pension income** can be transferred to your spouse.

**How to do it:** Fill out **form T1032** (federal) with your tax return each year. In Quebec, also complete **TP-1029.8.R-V**. The split is NOT automatic — you must renew it every year.

**Practical example — Michael ($85,000) and Claire ($25,000):**

Michael receives: employer pension $50,000 + RRIF $20,000 + CPP $15,000 = $85,000
Claire receives: CPP $16,000 + OAS $9,000 = $25,000

Michael can transfer up to 50% of ($50,000 + $20,000) = **$35,000** to Claire.

By transferring $20,000 → Michael declares $65,000, Claire declares $45,000.

Annual savings: ~$4,200/year.

### Strategy 2 — The Micro-RRIF at 65: Doubling the Pension Income Credit

Here's an underused trick that's particularly effective for couples where one partner has no employer pension.

The **pension income tax credit** applies to the first $2,000 of eligible pension income — credit value: approximately **$600** in combined federal/provincial tax savings.

**What many people don't know:** RRIF withdrawals *after age 65* qualify as eligible pension income. An RRSP, however, does not.

**The strategy:**
1. Convert $20,000–$30,000 of RRSP to a RRIF at age 65 (before the mandatory conversion at 71)
2. Withdraw ~$12,000/year from the RRIF
3. Split 50% with your spouse → each reports $6,000
4. **Both claim the pension income tax credit**
5. Result: two credits of ~$600 = **savings of ~$1,200** instead of $600

---

### Strategy 3 — CPP/QPP Pension Sharing

**An often-forgotten strategy — and very powerful.**

You can ask Service Canada (CPP) or Retraite Québec (QPP) to share your pensions between spouses. The pension isn't "given" — it's recalculated and divided based on years of shared life.

**CPP conditions (Service Canada):**
- Common-law or married partners for at least 1 year
- Both are 60 or older and receive (or are eligible for) CPP

**QPP conditions (Retraite Québec):**
- Married, civil union, or common-law for at least 3 years
- Both are 60 or older

**Potential savings:**
If one spouse receives the maximum (~$1,433/month) and the other receives nothing, sharing can generate up to **$4,583/year** in tax savings depending on respective marginal rates.

**Important:** This sharing is **permanent once approved**. Carefully analyze the long-term impacts before applying.

---

### Strategy 4 — Spousal RRSP (Plan While You're Still Working)

If you still have RRSP contribution room and are under 71, the higher-earning spouse can contribute to the partner's RRSP.

At retirement, it's the lower-income spouse who withdraws — paying tax at their lower rate.

**The 2 complete calendar years rule:**
If the spouse withdraws funds within the 2 *complete* calendar years following the contribution year, the contributor pays the tax.

**Example:** Contribution made in 2026 → withdrawal without attribution possible starting January 1, **2029** (2027 and 2028 are the two required complete calendar years).

## 🛡️ Income Splitting + OAS Protection: The Winning Combination

Income splitting doesn't just reduce taxes — it also protects your **Old Age Security (OAS)**.

In 2026, if your income exceeds **$95,323**, you lose 15 cents of OAS for every dollar above the threshold. Full OAS represents approximately **$8,808/year** at ages 65–74 and **$9,684/year** at 75+ (with the 10% boost).

By splitting pension income with your spouse, you reduce your individual taxable income and avoid this clawback.

**Example:**
- Marc, age 71: total income $100,000 → OAS clawback: ($100,000 – $95,323) × 15% = **$701/year** lost
- Marc transfers $15,000 to spouse Sylvie via T1032 → Marc's income: $85,000
- **Marc's OAS clawback: $0**
- Combined savings (tax + recovered OAS): **$2,500–$4,000/year**

## 📊 Other Income Splitting Methods

### Prescribed Rate Loan

The higher-income spouse lends money to the lower-income spouse at the **government prescribed interest rate** (revised quarterly). The lower-income spouse invests the sum — investment returns above the interest paid belong to them for tax purposes.

**Essential conditions:** The loan must be documented in writing, and interest must actually be paid by January 30 each subsequent year.

### Gift for Spouse's TFSA

Giving money to your spouse so they can contribute to their TFSA is **completely free of attribution rules**. Income in the TFSA belongs to the spouse who received the gift — and there's no tax on withdrawals. This is the simplest income-splitting method available.

### Gift to Adult Children's TFSA

You can give money to your adult children for their TFSA contributions. No attribution rules apply to gifts to adult children.

## ❌ Pitfalls to Avoid

### Pitfall 1: Attribution Rules on Non-TFSA Gifts

If you give money to your spouse to invest in a non-registered account, **investment income comes back to you for tax purposes** (attribution rules). Exception: TFSA.

### Pitfall 2: Splitting Without Calculating the Impact on the Receiving Spouse

If your spouse receives the Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) or other income-tested benefits, allocating more income to them could reduce their government benefits. Run the numbers before deciding.

### Pitfall 3: Forgetting the Form Each Year

Pension splitting (T1032) is **not automatic**. You must complete the form each year with your tax return. Forgetting means losing that year's savings.

### Pitfall 4: Early Withdrawal from Spousal RRSP

Respect the 2 complete calendar years rule — otherwise the tax reverts to the contributor, eliminating all benefit.

## ❓ Frequently Asked Questions

### "We have roughly equal incomes — is this useful?"

No, income splitting is only advantageous if one spouse has a significantly higher income. With balanced incomes, the savings are zero.

### "Can CPP/QPP pension sharing be cancelled?"

Yes, in cases of separation, divorce, or death. For stable couples, it's effectively a permanent decision. Analyze it carefully.

### "What happens if we separate?"

Pension splitting via T1032 has no effect on ownership — each keeps their pension. A spousal RRSP, however, belongs to the spouse who holds it. Separation may trigger sharing rights depending on your province.

### "Can I split TFSA income?"

No — TFSA withdrawals are not taxable. There's nothing to split, and that's precisely the TFSA's advantage.
