# Retirement Planning for the Self-Employed and Incorporated Business Owners in Quebec

You chose freedom: working for yourself, building your business, deciding your own hours and projects. But this freedom has a cost that's often underestimated: **you have no employer pension plan**. No RREGOP, no employer matching contributions, no pension fund.

This doesn't mean you can't have a comfortable retirement — quite the opposite. Self-employed workers and incorporated entrepreneurs often have access to retirement planning tools that are **more powerful** than those available to employees, provided they're used strategically.

This guide covers the key strategies for two main profiles: the **unincorporated self-employed** (who file a T1 return) and the **incorporated entrepreneur** (who operates through a corporation).

---

## 👤 Profile 1 — The Unincorporated Self-Employed

### Your Main Tool: The RRSP

The RRSP is the most effective retirement savings tool for the self-employed. Unlike employees who contribute 18% of their annual income up to the limit, the self-employed benefit from **RRSP room based on their net business income** — meaning your business deductions (expenses, depreciation) can reduce your RRSP room.

**Strategy**: Maximize your legitimate deductions, but ensure your net income is sufficient to generate good RRSP room.

#### RRSP Limit 2026

- **18% of earned income** from the previous year
- Maximum: **$33,810** (2026)
- Unused room accumulates indefinitely

**Example**: With $150,000 in net business income, your RRSP room is $27,000 (18% × $150,000) — less than the maximum.

### TFSA: Indispensable Complement

The TFSA is not limited by your business income. With a cumulative limit of **$109,000 in 2026** (for someone eligible since 2009), it's a powerful tool that complements the RRSP.

**RRSP vs TFSA Priority for Self-Employed**:
- Contribute to RRSP if your current marginal rate is high (45%+) and you anticipate a lower rate in retirement
- Contribute to TFSA if your income fluctuates and you want withdrawal flexibility without tax impact

### QPP Contributions (Quebec Pension Plan)

Unlike employees who share QPP contributions with their employer (50/50), self-employed workers **pay the full contribution** — essentially **twice** the normal contribution rate.

**2026 rate (estimated)**:
- Employee: ~5.4% of income up to the maximum pensionable earnings
- Self-employed: ~10.8% of the same income

**Impact**: Your higher QPP contributions generate larger future pension benefits — a form of "forced retirement savings" that protects you against yourself.

## 🏢 Profile 2 — The Incorporated Entrepreneur

Entrepreneurs who operate through a corporation have access to a far more sophisticated array of retirement tools — but also more complex ones.

### The Tax Advantage of Corporate Retained Earnings

When you leave profits inside your corporation, they're taxed at the **small business rate**:
- Federal: 9% on the first $500,000 of active income
- Quebec: approximately 3.2% on the first $500,000
- **Combined Quebec rate**: approximately 12.2%

Compared to your personal marginal rate that can reach 53.3% (on income above $200,000), keeping money in the corporation leaves you **approximately 4 times more** capital to invest.

### The Deferred Dividend: Long-Term Planning

The classic strategy for incorporated entrepreneurs preparing for retirement is to:
1. Accumulate profits in the corporation during active years
2. Only draw a minimal salary or dividend (based on personal needs)
3. At retirement, begin drawing dividends from the corporation at a lower marginal rate

**Advantage**: You have complete control over the **timing** and **amount** of your taxable income.

### The $50,000 Passive Income Rule

⚠️ **Important**: If your corporation earns more than **$50,000 in passive investment income** (interest, dividends, rents, capital gains) in a year, your Small Business Deduction (SBD) begins to erode. Above **$150,000 in passive income**, you lose the SBD entirely.

This creates tension: the more you accumulate in the corporation, the more you risk losing the SBD tax advantage.

**Solutions**:
- Favour equity investments that generate capital gains rather than interest
- Use exempt investments (exempt life insurance) to accumulate without declaring passive income
- Establish a separate holding company (holdco) distinct from the operating company

### The Holding Company (Holdco)

Creating a **holding company (holdco)** separate from your **operating company (opco)** is a common strategy:

1. The opco distributes its profits to the holdco via **tax-free inter-corporate dividends**
2. The holdco invests these profits
3. The holdco doesn't conduct active business, so no risk of its assets being exposed to the opco's creditors

This mechanism protects your accumulated wealth and optimizes estate planning.

## 🛠️ Retirement Tools Specific to Incorporated Owners

### 1. Owner's RRSP

An incorporated entrepreneur can draw a **salary** from their corporation and contribute to an RRSP on that salary (18% up to $33,810 maximum). This strategy:
- Generates a personal deduction on salary
- Creates additional RRSP room
- Allows optimizing salary vs dividend each year

### 2. Spousal RRSP

If your spouse has little or no income, contributing to a **spousal RRSP** allows you to:
- Claim the tax deduction yourself (at your high marginal rate)
- Create retirement income for your spouse (at their lower marginal rate)
- Effectively split income in retirement

**3-year rule**: RRSP spousal withdrawals are attributed back to you (the contributor) if the withdrawal occurs within the 3 calendar years following a contribution. Plan accordingly.

### 3. Individual Pension Plan (IPP)

An IPP is the equivalent of a defined benefit pension plan for a single shareholder. It allows contributing **more** than an RRSP through actuarial calculations, particularly for entrepreneurs over 40 with income over $100,000.

**Advantages**:
- Higher deductible contributions by the corporation
- Funding of actuarial deficits by the corporation
- Creditor protection (plan assets separate from corporation)
- Guaranteed retirement income

**Disadvantages**:
- Significant setup and administration costs ($5,000 to $15,000/year)
- Regulatory complexity
- Recommended only for entrepreneurs 45+ with stable, high income

### 4. Capital Dividend Account (CDA)

The CDA is not technically a retirement account, but plays an important role in retirement planning for incorporated owners. It accumulates the non-taxable portion of capital gains realized by the corporation (typically 50% of the gain).

At retirement, you can withdraw CDA amounts **tax-free** via capital dividend — a completely tax-exempt income source.

**Example**: Your corporation sells shares generating $200,000 in capital gains. $100,000 enters the CDA. At retirement, you pay yourself this $100,000 as a capital dividend: **no tax whatsoever**.

### 5. Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption (LCGE)

If you're a shareholder in a qualifying small business corporation (QSBC), you benefit from a lifetime exemption on capital gains when selling your shares:
- 2026 limit: **$1,250,000** (lifetime cumulative exemption — adjusted annually)

This exemption is one of the most powerful retirement tools for entrepreneurs: by selling your business at the right time, you can receive over a million dollars **completely tax-free**.

**Important**: Your business must meet eligibility criteria (assets actively used in a Canadian business, control criteria, etc.). Prior planning of **2 years or more** is recommended.

## 📋 Planning Timeline for the Entrepreneur

### 10-15 Years Before Retirement

- [ ] Evaluate whether an IPP makes sense for you
- [ ] Maximize RRSP contributions on salary each year
- [ ] Accumulate in the holdco if you have excess profits
- [ ] Plan the use of the LCGE on share sales

### 5-10 Years Before Retirement

- [ ] Verify your shares' LCGE eligibility criteria
- [ ] Maximize your TFSA and your spouse's TFSA
- [ ] Begin planning business succession/transfer
- [ ] Evaluate whether exempt life insurance makes sense in the holdco

### Within 3 Years Before Retirement

- [ ] Prepare the RRSP meltdown (if share sale generates large income, postpone RRSP withdrawals)
- [ ] Plan post-retirement dividend structure from the corporation
- [ ] Calculate your QPP entitlements and plan optimal claiming age

## ⚠️ The 5 Most Costly Entrepreneurial Mistakes

**1. Waiting too long to plan**
Retirement tax planning must begin **years** before departure. Some structures (IPP, estate freeze) require several years of preparation to be effective.

**2. Confusing cash flow with retirement planning**
Money in the corporation is not "your" retirement money until it's been distributed (with the taxes attached). Many entrepreneurs underestimate the personal tax payable upon distribution.

**3. Under-utilizing the TFSA**
Even with a corporation, your personal TFSA remains an essential retirement tool — completely tax-sheltered.

**4. Not planning for both spouses**
If your spouse is involved in the business or has low income, not optimizing income splitting can cost tens of thousands of additional dollars in taxes.

**5. Neglecting corporate life insurance**
Life insurance held in the holding company can accumulate cash values tax-sheltered and fund the CDA — a powerful accumulation strategy for high-income earners.

## ❓ FAQ

**Q: I'm self-employed. Am I eligible for Employment Insurance if I stop working?**
Generally, no. Self-employed workers don't contribute to EI and don't have access to it (except those who voluntarily participated for certain special benefits like parental leave).

**Q: What's the difference between salary and dividend as income from my corporation?**
Salary generates RRSP room and QPP contributions, but is taxed as ordinary income. A dividend isn't subject to CPP/QPP contributions, doesn't create RRSP room, and may be taxed differently. The optimal mix varies each year based on your objectives.

**Q: My business is valued at $2 million. How do I maximize the LCGE on sale?**
If you're married and your spouse has eligible shares, each of you can use your individual LCGE ($1,250,000 each), potentially allowing up to $2,500,000 in exemption. An estate freeze and share issuance to the spouse must be planned years in advance.

**Q: Can I transfer my RRSP into my corporation?**
No. The RRSP is a personal account that cannot be transferred into a corporation. At retirement, you'll convert your RRSP to a RRIF and take taxable withdrawals.

## 📋 Summary

Retirement planning for a self-employed worker or incorporated entrepreneur in Quebec requires proactive, tailored planning:

- **Self-employed**: maximize RRSP + TFSA + QPP contributions
- **Incorporated**: leverage the tax rate differential, holdco, CDA, and LCGE
- **Advanced tools** (IPP, exempt life insurance, estate freeze) are worth the investment from age 40-45 with high income
- Work with a **team**: Certified Financial Planner + CPA + notary
