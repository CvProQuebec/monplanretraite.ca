# Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS): Are You Eligible?

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 10 minutes*

**GIS can be worth up to $13,200 per year in tax-free income. Yet thousands of eligible Canadians never apply. Here's how to know if you qualify, how to calculate your amount, and above all, how to protect it.**

---

## What is the Guaranteed Income Supplement?

GIS is a monthly amount the Government of Canada pays **on top of** your Old Age Security (OAS) pension if your income is modest.

**What makes it exceptional:**
- ✅ **Tax-free** — you keep every dollar
- ✅ **Indexed 4 times per year** for inflation
- ✅ **Paid for life** as long as you qualify
- ✅ **Automatically renewed** if you file your tax return each year

---

## Are You Eligible?

### The 3 Basic Conditions

1. **Already receiving OAS** (or have an accepted OAS application)
2. **Be 65 or older**
3. **Have low income** (based on the thresholds below)

### 2026 Income Thresholds (Approximate)

Thresholds are indexed quarterly. The values below are estimates for 2026:

| Situation | Maximum Annual Income (excl. OAS) |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| Single, widowed, divorced, or separated | ~$22,800 |
| Couple — both receive OAS | ~$29,808 (combined) |
| Couple — only one spouse receives OAS | ~$54,500 (combined) |

> If your income slightly exceeds these thresholds, you will still receive a partial amount — GIS decreases gradually, it doesn't disappear all at once.

---

## How Much Can You Receive?

### Approximate 2026 Maximum Amounts

| Situation | Monthly Maximum | Annual Maximum |
|-----------|----------------|----------------|
| Single person | ~$1,100 | ~$13,200 |
| Couple (each) | ~$660 | ~$7,920 each |

**These amounts are tax-free** — they are net money in your pocket with no impact on your other credits.

### How the Calculation Works

GIS decreases based on your other income (excluding OAS).

**For a single person:**
- You lose **$1 of GIS** for every **$2 of other income**

**For a couple:**
- You lose **$1 of GIS** for every **$4 of combined income**

---

## What Income Counts (and What Doesn't)?

### Income That Reduces Your GIS ✅

- RRSP or RRIF withdrawals
- Employer pension
- QPP or CPP
- Employment income (if you still work)
- Investment income (interest, dividends)
- Rental income
- Capital gains

### Income That Does NOT Count ❌

- Your Old Age Security pension (OAS)
- Your TFSA withdrawals
- GIS itself
- Cash gifts from family members

> 💡 **Key point:** The TFSA is your best tool for funding expenses without reducing your GIS.

## Concrete Examples

### Example 1 — Simone, 67, single

- OAS: ~$727/month (doesn't count)
- QPP: $400/month = $4,800/year
- Small employer pension: $200/month = $2,400/year
- **Total other income: $7,200/year**

**GIS calculation:**
- GIS maximum: ~$1,100/month
- Reduction: $7,200 ÷ 2 = $3,600/year = $300/month
- **Simone's GIS: $1,100 − $300 = $800/month**

Simone receives **$800/month** of tax-free GIS — **$9,600/year** net.

### Example 2 — Robert and Denise, both 66

- Both receive OAS (~$727/month — doesn't count)
- Robert's QPP: $800/month = $9,600/year
- Denise's QPP: $300/month = $3,600/year
- **Combined income: $13,200/year**

**GIS calculation:**
- GIS maximum each: ~$660/month
- Reduction: $13,200 ÷ 4 = $3,300/year = $275/month each
- **Each person's GIS: $660 − $275 = $385/month**

Robert and Denise each receive **$385/month** of tax-free GIS.

### Example 3 — Marcel, 63, planning ahead

Marcel has $100,000 in his RRSP and will receive a small employer pension of $300/month at 65.

**His calculation:** If he converts his entire RRSP to a RRIF at 65 and withdraws ~$10,000/year, plus his pension of $3,600/year = **$13,600/year** in income — he just barely exceeds the GIS threshold.

**His smart strategy:**
- Gradually transfers RRSP to TFSA between ages 60 and 65 (paying tax at low rates)
- At 65, his taxable income is under $8,000/year
- He receives **~$700/month in GIS** for several years
- He uses his TFSA to cover extra expenses (no impact on GIS)
- **Result: $8,400/year in tax-free GIS** for 5–10 years

## 5 Strategies to Protect Your GIS

### Strategy 1: Prioritize Your TFSA for Expenses

TFSA withdrawals don't count as income. If you need extra money, draw from your TFSA before touching your RRIF or other taxable investments.

### Strategy 2: Minimize RRIF Withdrawals

A large RRIF withdrawal can eliminate your GIS for an entire year. If you need a large sum (renovation, travel), think about how to spread it out or use other sources.

### Strategy 3: Spread Capital Gains Over Multiple Years

If you need to sell investments, spread the sales over several years to prevent a big capital gain year from wiping out your GIS.

### Strategy 4: Coordinate with Your Spouse

If you're a couple, look at which spouse should receive which income. In some cases, concentrating taxable income on one spouse (the one who doesn't receive OAS) can preserve the other's GIS.

### Strategy 5: Plan Your Income BEFORE Age 65

The best time to protect your GIS is **before** you receive it. If you can transfer savings from RRSP to TFSA between ages 55 and 65 (paying tax at low rates), you reduce future taxable income and maximize GIS eligibility.

## Important Special Cases

### If You're in a Couple and Only One Spouse is 65

If you're between 60 and 64 and your spouse receives OAS and has low income, you might be eligible for the **Allowance** (~$1,430/month approx. 2026).

If you're widowed between 60 and 64, you might be eligible for the **Allowance for the Survivor** (~$1,700/month approx. 2026). These benefits are also tax-free and adjusted quarterly.

### If You're a Recent Immigrant

If you've lived less than 40 years in Canada, your OAS will be reduced proportionally. But your GIS might be higher to partially compensate.

### If You Live Abroad More Than 6 Months per Year

Generally, you lose your GIS if you spend more than 6 months abroad per year. A few exceptions exist — check with Service Canada.

### If Your Income Changes Mid-Year

GIS is calculated based on your **previous year's** income. If you've had an exceptional event (income loss, death of spouse), you can ask Service Canada to recalculate your GIS based on your current income rather than last year's.

## The 4 Costly Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Never Applying

The government does **not** automatically give you GIS. You must make an initial application, then file your tax return each year for renewal.

### Mistake 2: A Large RRSP/RRIF Withdrawal Without Planning

A $20,000 RRSP withdrawal can reduce or eliminate your GIS for an entire year, representing a loss of $6,600–$13,200. Planning prevents this trap.

### Mistake 3: Ignoring Combined Couple Income

Many couples think about their individual income. But it's the **combined** income that determines GIS. A spouse with decent income can reduce or eliminate the other's GIS.

### Mistake 4: Not Filing a Tax Return Each Year

GIS is automatically renewed **only if** you file your tax return every year. Even with very low income, file your return.

## Who Receives GIS?

**Women represent 60% of GIS recipients.** They have often had interrupted careers (maternity, caregiving), lower wages, and less retirement savings.

70% of divorced or widowed women over 75 receive GIS — a reality that underscores the importance of planning tailored to each situation.

---

## How to Apply?

1. **First application:**
   - Online through My Service Canada Account (canada.ca)
   - By phone: 1-800-277-9914
   - In person at a Service Canada office

2. **Required documents:**
   - Social insurance number
   - Previous year's tax return
   - Statements of all your income

3. **Renewal:**
   - Automatic each July if you file your tax return
   - Your new amount is based on the previous year's income

---

## Quick Eligibility Checklist

Before applying, answer these questions:

- ☐ Do I receive OAS (or have I applied)?
- ☐ What is my annual income excluding OAS?
- ☐ Am I under the threshold for my situation (~$22,800 single / ~$29,808 couple)?
- ☐ Can I reduce my taxable income by using TFSA instead of RRIF?
- ☐ Have I filed my income tax return this year?

If you answered "yes" to the first three, apply now — don't leave money on the table.

---

## Conclusion: Don't Leave This Money on the Table

GIS can represent more than **$13,200 per year** in net income. That's significant for anyone with modest retirement income.

**Key Points:**

✅ Apply at 65 if your income is modest
✅ File your tax return every year — even with low income
✅ Use your TFSA for extra expenses to protect your GIS
✅ Plan your RRIF withdrawals to avoid eliminating GIS
✅ If your income changes, contact Service Canada promptly
✅ If you're widowed between 60–64, apply for the Allowance for the Survivor

**The most important message:** If your income (excluding OAS) is under ~$22,800/year, check your eligibility. Thousands of eligible people never apply.
