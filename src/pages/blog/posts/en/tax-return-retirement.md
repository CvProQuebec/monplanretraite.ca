# Retirement Taxes and Tax Return: The Complete 2026 Guide

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 10 minutes*

**Retirement changes your tax situation completely. New income sources, special credits, and one major trap to avoid — the OAS clawback. Here's everything you need to know to prepare your tax return and plan your taxes for 2026.**

---

## How Your Tax Situation Changes in Retirement

When you worked, your income came from one source. In retirement, your money can come from several different places. Each income source has its own tax rules. Understanding them is essential to avoid unpleasant surprises.

---

## Your New Retirement Income Sources

### 1. Government Benefits

**Old Age Security (OAS)**
- You're entitled at age 65 if you've lived in Canada for at least 10 years
- 2026 amounts: approximately **$727/month** (ages 65–74) or **$800/month** (age 75 and over)
- The 10% increase at 75 is automatic — no application required
- **Important:** This pension is taxable and can be clawed back if your income is too high

**Quebec Pension Plan (QPP)**
- Based on your years of contributions while working
- 2026 maximum: approximately **$1,433/month** (at 65, with maximum contributions)
- Can start at 60 (reduced) or delay to 70 (up to +42%)

**Canada Pension Plan (CPP)**
- For residents outside Quebec
- 2026 maximum: approximately **$1,395/month** (at 65)
- Same deferral options as QPP

**Important:** All government pensions are fully taxable. They add to your other income to calculate your taxes.

### 2. Employer Pension

If you worked for a company that had a pension plan:
- This pension is fully taxable
- It qualifies as "pension income" (important for certain tax credits)
- It can be split with your spouse in certain cases

### 3. RRSP Becoming RRIF

**What changes at age 71**
Before 71, your money can stay in your RRSP tax-sheltered. But at 71, you must convert your RRSP to a RRIF (Registered Retirement Income Fund).

**Mandatory withdrawals**
Once you have a RRIF:
- You must withdraw a minimum amount each year
- All withdrawals are fully taxable
- The minimum percentage increases as you age

**Minimum withdrawal examples:**
- At age 65: approximately 4% of your RRIF
- At age 75: approximately 5.4% of your RRIF
- At age 85: approximately 8.5% of your RRIF

### 4. Personal Investments

Money invested outside your RRSP:
- Interest is 100% taxable
- Dividends receive favourable tax treatment
- Capital gains are only 50% taxable

## New Tax Rules That Apply in Retirement

### You May Pay Less Tax

**Why?**
- Your income is often lower than before
- You qualify for new tax credits
- You can split certain income with your spouse
- In 2026, the first federal tax bracket dropped to **14%** (from 15% in prior years)

**Special tax credits for retirees:**
- **Age Credit**: For anyone 65 and over
- **Pension Income Credit**: For private pensions and RRIF withdrawals (up to $2,000 protected)
- **Caregiver Credit**: If you care for a family member
- **Medical Expense Credit**: For qualifying health costs

### Income Splitting: A Major Tax Saver

**How it works**
Our tax system uses graduated brackets. The more you earn, the higher your rate. By splitting income with your spouse, you may pay less overall.

**Concrete example:**
- One person with $40,000 income pays approximately $5,991 in tax
- Two people at $20,000 each pay approximately $1,370 total
- **Savings: $4,621 per year!**

**Which income can be split?**

At age 65 and over:
- RRIF income
- Employer pension annuities
- Life annuities

At any age:
- Disability pensions
- Certain insurance annuities

**How to do it:** File form T1032 with your tax return. You can split up to 50% of eligible income.

### QPP/CPP Pension Sharing

You and your spouse can share your QPP/CPP pensions with Retraite Québec or Service Canada.

**Conditions:**
- Both must be 60 or older
- Must be married or common-law for at least 3 years
- At least one must be receiving QPP/CPP

**Note:** This is different from pension income splitting. It's a permanent change to how your pensions are paid.

## Tax Slips You'll Receive

### T4A(OAS) — Old Age Security
Shows how much OAS you received. Enter this amount in your return.

### T4A(P) — QPP or CPP
Shows your QPP or CPP pension amounts.

### T4RIF — RRIF
If you have a RRIF, this slip shows:
- How much you withdrew
- How much tax was withheld at source

### T4A — Employer Pension
If you receive a pension from your former employer, this slip shows the amounts received.

---

## Where to Enter Your Income

### If You're 65 or Older

**Line 11500 — Eligible Pension Income:**
Enter here:
- Your RRIF withdrawals
- Your employer pension
- Your QPP/CPP pension

**Why it matters:** This income qualifies you for the pension income credit.

### If You're Under 65

**Line 13000 — Other Income:**
Most retirement income goes here, except pensions received due to a spouse's death.

> 💡 **Tip:** Even before 65, converting a small portion of your RRSP to a RRIF unlocks access to the pension income credit (~$2,000 protected). Ask your financial planner.

## ⚠️ The OAS Clawback — The Most Costly Retirement Tax Trap

### What Is It?

If your income is too high, the government recovers part or all of your OAS pension.

**2026 threshold: $95,323**
**Rate:** You repay 15¢ for every dollar above the threshold.

> ⚠️ **Warning:** Many older guides still show $86,912 or $90,997 as the clawback threshold. These figures are no longer current. The 2026 threshold is **$95,323**.

**2026 Example:**
- Your income: $100,000
- Excess above threshold: $100,000 − $95,323 = **$4,677**
- Clawback: $4,677 × 15% = **$701/year**

**High income example:**
- Income of $110,000
- Excess: $14,677
- Clawback: **$2,202/year** — nearly your entire OAS!

### How to Avoid the Clawback

**Monitor your RRIF withdrawals:** The more you withdraw, the higher your income.

**Use your TFSA:** Withdrawals from your TFSA don't count as income — your best weapon against the clawback.

**Plan with your spouse:** Income splitting can help keep you under the threshold.

**The RRSP Meltdown Strategy (ages 60–70):** One of the most powerful long-term strategies. Before your government pensions stack up, your taxable income is often in lower brackets. This is the ideal time to gradually withdraw from your RRSP (~$12,000–$20,000/year), pay tax at low rates, and reduce the size of your RRIF at 72. Smaller RRIF = smaller mandatory withdrawals = income stays below $95,323.

## Tax Planning Strategies for Retirement

### 1. The RRSP Meltdown (Ages 60–70)

**The principle:** Gradually withdraw from your RRSP in the years before government pensions begin, when your taxable income is lowest.

**Example:** Marie has $200,000 in her RRSP at age 60. She withdraws $15,000/year for 10 years. At 71, her RRIF contains only $80,000 (including growth). Her mandatory withdrawals at 72 are approximately $3,440/year — well below the OAS clawback threshold.

**Why this works:**
- You pay tax on withdrawals at low rates (before pensions stack up)
- Your RRIF is smaller at 72 — mandatory withdrawals are smaller
- You protect your OAS for decades

### 2. Use Your TFSA Wisely

**TFSA withdrawals are invisible to the CRA.** They don't count as income, don't affect your clawback risk, and don't impact GIS eligibility.

**Strategy:** If your RRIF withdrawals risk pushing you above $95,323, cover extra expenses from your TFSA.

**2026 TFSA cumulative limit:** $109,000 (if you've never contributed since eligibility).

### 3. Delay OAS if Beneficial

You can wait until age 70 to start your OAS. Each month of delay after 65 increases your pension by **0.6%**.

**If you wait 5 years (until 70): +36% for life!**
- $727 → $988/month (indexed for life)

This strategy makes sense if your income between 65 and 70 already risks triggering the clawback.

### 4. Convert a Small RRSP to RRIF at 65

Even before the mandatory conversion at 71, converting a small portion of your RRSP to a RRIF at 65 gives you access to the **pension income credit** — protecting up to $2,000 of pension income from tax entirely.

### 5. Spread Capital Gains Over Multiple Years

Capital gains count as taxable income. Spread investment sales over several years to avoid a high-income year that triggers the clawback.

## Preparing Your Retirement Tax Return

### Documents to Gather

Before starting, collect:
- All your tax slips (T4A(OAS), T4A(P), T4RIF, T4A)
- Your non-registered investment statements
- Medical expense receipts
- Charitable donation receipts

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Impact |
|---------|--------|
| Forgetting to apply for OAS | Missed months of benefits |
| Using old threshold ($86,912 or $90,997) | Wrong tax planning |
| Ignoring TFSA as an anti-clawback tool | Hundreds to thousands lost per year |
| Large RRIF withdrawal without planning | OAS clawback triggered |
| Missing the pension income credit | Up to ~$440 left unclaimed |
| Insufficient withholding on pensions | Surprise tax bill in April |

### Withholding Tax on RRSP Withdrawals

If you withdraw from your RRSP (before converting to RRIF):
- Under $5,000: 19% withheld (Quebec)
- $5,001–$15,000: 24% withheld
- Over $15,000: 29% withheld

**Important:** These withholdings are often insufficient. You may owe more at year-end.

### Use Software or a Professional

For retirees, filing can be more complex:
- Multiple income sources
- Special tax credits
- Splitting strategies
- Clawback calculations

Don't hesitate to use recognized tax software or consult an accountant.

## Important Dates

**April 30:** Deadline to file your return and pay any balance owed.

**Important:** Even if you owe no tax, file every year to:
- Receive your GST/solidarity credits
- Maintain your government benefits (GIS is automatically renewed each July if you file)
- Keep your file up to date with the CRA

## Planning for Next Year

### Adjust Your Tax Withholdings

If you owed a lot of tax this year:
- Ask to increase withholdings on your pensions
- Consider making quarterly installment payments

### Plan Your RRIF Withdrawals

**Withdraw only the required minimum** if you don't need more. This:
- Reduces your taxes
- Avoids OAS clawback (2026 threshold: $95,323)
- Keeps your money growing tax-sheltered

### Think About Splitting

If your spouse has lower income:
- Use pension income splitting (form T1032)
- Contribute to their TFSA rather than yours
- Plan your withdrawals together

## Summary: Key Points for 2026

✅ Your retirement income is generally taxable (except TFSA withdrawals)
✅ You qualify for new tax credits at age 65
✅ Income splitting can save thousands per year
✅ **OAS clawback threshold 2026: $95,323** (NOT $86,912 or $90,997!)
✅ TFSA is your best weapon against the clawback
✅ RRSP meltdown between ages 60–70 protects OAS at 72
✅ File your tax return every year — even with low income
✅ The 10% OAS bonus at 75 is automatic — no action needed
