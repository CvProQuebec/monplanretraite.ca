# FHSA: The Complete Guide for Parents of First-Time Home Buyers

Since its launch in 2023, the **FHSA** (First Home Savings Account) is one of the most advantageous registered accounts ever created in Canada. For parents of a young adult aspiring to become a homeowner, understanding the FHSA is essential — not only to help their child use it effectively, but also to plan their financial assistance in the most tax-efficient way possible.

This guide covers everything a parent needs to know about the FHSA: the rules, savings strategies, pitfalls to avoid, and how your financial support can be structured to maximize the tax advantage.

---

## 🏠 What Is the FHSA?

The FHSA combines the advantages of both an **RRSP** and a **TFSA** in a single account:

- **Contributions are tax-deductible** (like an RRSP) — reducing your child's taxable income
- **Qualifying withdrawals are tax-free** for the purchase of a first property (like a TFSA)
- **Growth inside the account** is tax-sheltered

It's therefore the only Canadian account that offers a deduction at contribution AND exemption at withdrawal.

### FHSA Key Numbers (2026)

| Parameter | Rule |
|---|---|
| Maximum annual contribution | $8,000 |
| Maximum cumulative contribution | $40,000 |
| Maximum account lifespan | 15 years (from opening) |
| Carry-forward of room | 1 year maximum (up to $16,000 max in year 2 if nothing in year 1) |
| Minimum opening age | 18 |
| Canadian residency required | Yes |

---

## ✅ Who Can Open an FHSA?

For your adult child to open an FHSA, they must:

1. Be a **Canadian tax resident**
2. Be at least **18 years old**
3. Not have been the owner of a **qualifying home** or have lived in a home owned by their spouse during the **preceding 4 years**

> **Important**: first-buyer status is assessed at the **time of opening the account**, not at the time of purchase. Your child must be eligible from the start.

---

## 💰 How Can Parents Help?

### Option 1: Give Money to Fund Contributions

Parents can give their child money to contribute to their own FHSA. It's simple and legal. The tax deduction on the contribution belongs to the child (not the parent), but the indirect benefit is that:

- Your help reduces your child's taxes
- The gifted money grows tax-sheltered inside the account
- At withdrawal, neither your child nor you pay tax on these amounts

### Option 2: Family Loan

You can lend money to your child to allow them to contribute to the FHSA, even if they don't have the cash available yet. The tax deduction your child will receive can serve to gradually repay the loan.

### Option 3: Open the Account as Early as Possible

The FHSA has a 15-year lifespan. If your child doesn't buy a home within this period, the funds must be transferred to an RRSP (without affecting RRSP contribution room) or withdrawn (taxable as ordinary income).

Encourage your child to **open the account as soon as they turn 18** even if they can only contribute a little — because the 15-year clock starts when the account is opened. The earlier they open it, the more time they'll have.

> 💡 **Strategy for retired parents**: If you have a well-funded TFSA, you can withdraw a portion to give to your child to fund their FHSA. This TFSA withdrawal generates no tax for you, and your TFSA room regenerates the following year.

- At 5% return over 5 years: approximately **$88,400** in potential down payment (not counting the tax savings on contributions)

## 🔄 FHSA + Home Buyers' Plan: A Powerful Combination

The FHSA can be combined with the **HBP (Home Buyers' Plan)** from the RRSP. A first-time buyer can use both simultaneously:

- **FHSA**: up to $40,000 tax-free at withdrawal
- **HBP (RRSP)**: up to $35,000 temporarily withdrawn from RRSP, to be repaid over 15 years

**Combined potential down payment**:
- Single buyer: $40,000 (FHSA) + $35,000 (HBP) = **$75,000**
- Couple (two first-time buyers): $80,000 (FHSA × 2) + $70,000 (HBP × 2) = **$150,000**

This is an extraordinarily powerful combination, particularly in expensive real estate markets like Montreal and other major cities.

## 🎓 Special Strategy: Open the FHSA at 18, Even With No Money

One of the most underused strategies is to **open the account as early as possible** to start the 15-year clock, even without contributing immediately.

**Why?**
- If your child doesn't buy before age 30 (which is common), they will have opened their account at 18, leaving 12 more years available
- The contribution room from year one ($8,000) can be carried forward for only one year, so contributing $16,000 in year two
- Money has more time to grow tax-sheltered

---

## 🔍 What Parents Need to Know About Qualifying Withdrawals

For the FHSA withdrawal to be tax-free, your child must meet several conditions at the time of purchase:

1. **Be a first-time buyer** (not owned a home in the last 4 years)
2. Have a **written purchase agreement** before October 1 of the year following the withdrawal
3. Intend to **occupy the property** as principal residence within the year following acquisition
4. Be a **Canadian resident** at the time of withdrawal

> ⚠️ If your child withdraws the money for any reason other than purchasing a first property, the withdrawal is taxable. Ensure conditions are respected.

## ❓ Frequently Asked Questions from Parents

**Q: Can I contribute directly to my child's FHSA?**
No. You cannot contribute directly to someone else's account. You must **give** the money to your child, who then contributes in their own name. The tax deduction belongs to them.

**Q: Can my child contribute to the FHSA if they're studying and have little income?**
Yes. Unlike an RRSP, there's no requirement to have earned income to contribute to an FHSA. However, if their income is low, the tax deduction will have little immediate value. They can defer the deduction claim to a future year when their income is higher — unused contributions can be carried forward.

**Q: My child had a house but sold it 3 years ago. Can they open an FHSA?**
No. The rule requires not having owned a principal residence during the **preceding 4 years**. If the sale was 3 years ago, they wouldn't be eligible today, but would be next year (completing 4 full years).

**Q: What if my child never buys a house? What happens to the money?**
The money can be transferred **tax-free** to an RRSP, without affecting your child's RRSP contribution room. This is an advantageous exit strategy — the FHSA essentially becomes a "super-RRSP" with an upfront deduction on contributions.

**Q: Can the FHSA be used for a duplex or rental property?**
Only if your child plans to live there as their primary residence. The purchase of a purely rental property is not eligible.

**Q: My child is a Canadian citizen but lives in the US. Can they open an FHSA?**
No. They must be a **Canadian tax resident** at the time of opening the account and at the time of the qualifying withdrawal.

## ⚠️ Pitfalls to Avoid

**1. Forgetting the one-year-only carry-forward**
The FHSA only allows carrying forward one year of contribution room (not unlimited like the RRSP). If your child hasn't contributed for 3 years, they cannot recover that lost room.

**2. Contributing to the FHSA when room is exhausted**
Any excess contribution is subject to a **1% per month penalty** — like RRSP or TFSA over-contributions. Your child must rigorously track their contribution room.

**3. Not taking advantage of the deduction now**
If your child has taxable income this year, they should claim the deduction on their return — and receive a tax refund they can reinvest in the account.

**4. Inadvertently closing the FHSA**
If your child closes the account without using it for a home purchase and without transferring to an RRSP, the withdrawn amounts are fully taxable as income.

## 📋 Summary: The Action Plan for Parents and Young Adults

The FHSA is the most powerful savings tool for first-time buyers in Canada. In summary:

- **Open the account as early as possible**, even with little money, to start the 15-year clock
- **Contribute $8,000/year** and claim the tax deduction each year
- Parents can **give money** to their child to fund contributions — no tax for anyone
- **Combine with the HBP** to maximize the down payment
- **Transfer to RRSP** if the purchase doesn't happen before the end of 15 years
