# Delaying Government Pensions: Why It Can Pay Off

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 8 minutes*

**Delaying your government pensions (QPP/CPP and OAS) is often presented as a simple monthly-amount tradeoff. It's actually a complete tax strategy that can be worth $60,000 to $120,000 over your retirement — if you're in good health and have other income to cover the waiting period.**

---

## 🏛️ Recap: The Permanent Enhancements

Every month you wait, the government increases your pension permanently and with inflation protection.

| Pension | Before 65 | After 65 | Maximum |
|---------|----------|----------|---------|
| **QPP/CPP** | −0.6%/month | +0.7%/month | +58.8% at 72 (QPP) / +42% at 70 (CPP) |
| **OAS** | — (not before 65) | +0.6%/month | +36% at 70 |

These enhancements are **permanent** — you keep them for life — and **indexed** — they increase with inflation every year.

**2026 Reference Amounts:**

| Pension | Amount at 65 | Amount at 70 |
|---------|-------------|-------------|
| QPP (max) | ~$1,433/month | ~$2,035/month |
| CPP (max) | ~$1,395/month | ~$1,981/month |
| OAS | ~$727/month | ~$988/month (+36%) |
| OAS at 75+ | ~$800/month (automatic) | — |

---

## 📊 5 Reasons Why Waiting Can Pay Off

### Reason 1: A Larger Guaranteed Income for Life

A government pension is money you receive every month, rain or shine, regardless of market conditions. The bigger it is, the better your security.

A retiree who takes their QPP at 70 instead of 65 receives **$602/month more** for the rest of their life. With indexation, this gap only grows with the years.

### Reason 2: Longevity Insurance

It's the guarantee you can never "spend down." If you live to 92, a pension of $2,035 is worth far more over 22 years than a pension of $1,433 over 27 years.

**Simple calculation:**
- QPP at 65 to age 92 = $1,433 × 324 months = **$464,292**
- QPP at 70 to age 92 = $2,035 × 264 months = **$537,240**
- **Difference: $72,948** (before indexation, which widens the gap further)

### Reason 3: Inflation Protection

Government pensions are indexed to inflation (CPI). A higher pension protects you better against rising living costs.

At 3% annual inflation, a pension of $2,035 grows by $61 in the first year. A pension of $1,433 only grows by $43. The gap widens every year.

### Reason 4: Tax Optimization — The Most Underestimated Reason

Between ages 60 and 70, while you wait for your pension, your taxable income is often lower. This is the ideal window to **melt down your RRSP** in low tax brackets.

By gradually withdrawing from your RRSP ($12,000–$20,000/year) during this period, you:
- Reduce mandatory minimum RRIF withdrawals at age 72
- Reduce the risk of the OAS clawback (2026 threshold: **$95,323**)
- Stay in favourable tax brackets even when QPP/CPP and OAS are added later

**See the complete strategy:** [The Smart Order to Withdraw Your Money in Retirement](/posts/en/smart-withdrawal-order)

### Reason 5: Surviving Spouse Protection

At your death, your spouse receives a survivor's pension. The higher your own pension during your lifetime, the better protected your spouse is if they outlive you — particularly important if you are the primary earner in the household.

## ⚖️ The Real Break-Even Points

| Decision | Break-Even Point | Current Life Expectancy |
|----------|-----------------|------------------------|
| QPP/CPP: wait from 60 to 65 | **~age 74** | Men 87 / Women 89 |
| QPP/CPP: wait from 65 to 70 | **~age 82** | Men 87 / Women 89 |
| OAS: wait from 65 to 70 | **~age 81** | Men 87 / Women 89 |

For a healthy 65-year-old Canadian, approximately **half** will live beyond age 90. The odds strongly favour deferral for those who have the means to wait.

## 💡 How to Fund the Waiting Period

This is the real practical question. Here are the sources to consider for covering your income between ages 60 and 70:

**Sources to prioritize (from most to least tax-advantageous):**

1. **Part-time work** — if you want to and can
2. **TFSA** — tax-free withdrawals, ideal for plugging gaps
3. **Gradual RRSP withdrawals** — take advantage of low brackets (RRSP meltdown)
4. **Non-registered investments** — income partially taxable depending on the type
5. **Employer pension** — if you have one, it may cover a large portion

**What to avoid:**
- Taking your QPP/CPP and OAS early just to avoid drawing on savings, when those savings will be taxed at higher rates later anyway

---

## 🚫 When Delaying Is NOT the Right Solution

Deferral is not universal. Here's when it makes more sense to take your pensions without waiting:

| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Poor health, reduced life expectancy | Take as soon as possible |
| No other income, need money now | Take at 60–65 |
| Less than $150,000 in total savings | Take at 65 |
| Widowed, living alone on low income | Evaluate OAS at 65 |

## 🎯 A Practical 3-Step Strategy

### Step 1: Calculate Your Income from 60 to 70 Without Government Pensions

List what income you'll have if you wait:
- Employer pension (if applicable)
- Planned RRSP/TFSA withdrawals
- Investment income
- Part-time work

If the total covers your needs with a reasonable cushion, you can wait.

### Step 2: Estimate the Tax Impact at Age 72

Project your income at 72 with mandatory RRIF withdrawals, your employer pension, your QPP/CPP, and OAS.

If your projected income exceeds $85,000–$90,000, the RRSP meltdown + pension deferral combination is almost certainly the right strategy.

### Step 3: Check Your Official Statement

Before deciding, verify your estimated personal pension:
- **QPP:** Mon dossier at retraitequebec.gouv.qc.ca
- **CPP:** My Service Canada Account (canada.ca)

Your actual amount may differ significantly from the maximum depending on your contribution history.

## ✅ The Key Takeaways

Delaying your government pensions means:

**✓ More money** — a permanent, indexed enhancement
**✓ Less tax** — the window to melt down your RRSP at low rates
**✓ More security** — protection against longevity and inflation
**✓ Better protection for your spouse** — a stronger survivor's pension

And it's not just for the wealthy: even with moderate savings, orchestrating the right withdrawal sequence can make a significant difference.
