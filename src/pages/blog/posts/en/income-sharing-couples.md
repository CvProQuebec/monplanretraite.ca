# How to Share Retirement Income and Save on Taxes

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 8 minutes*

**In retirement, if one spouse earns significantly more than the other, you're probably paying more tax than necessary. Here's how to organize your income so each person pays their fair share — and keep thousands of extra dollars in your household.**

---

## 💡 The Core Idea: Income Balance

Canada's tax system is progressive — the more one individual earns, the higher their marginal rate. Two incomes of $45,000 each cost far less in tax than one income of $90,000 and zero for the other.

**Illustrative example 2026:**

| Situation | Estimated Couple's Total Tax |
|-----------|------------------------------|
| Pierre earns $80,000, Marie earns $20,000 | ~$14,500 |
| Pierre and Marie each earn $50,000 | ~$9,800 |
| **Savings with balance** | **~$4,700/year** |

## 📋 The 3 Main Ways to Share Retirement Income

### Method 1 — Pension Income Splitting (Form T1032)

This is the simplest and most widely used method by retired couples.

**What you can share:**
- RRIF withdrawals ✓
- Defined benefit employer pensions ✓
- Life annuities from registered plans ✓
- Up to **50%** of this income can be attributed to your spouse

**What you cannot share with this method:**
- CPP/QPP ✗ (see Method 2)
- OAS ✗
- Investment income ✗

**How it works in practice:**
1. You calculate your eligible pension income
2. You choose the amount to share (up to 50%)
3. Your spouse reports this amount on their return
4. You deduct this amount on your return
5. Result: your income drops, your spouse's rises — the couple's total tax decreases

**How to do it:** Complete form **T1032** (federal) with your tax return each year. In Quebec, also complete **TP-1029.8.R-V**. Must be renewed every year — it is NOT automatic.

The more pronounced the income gap, the greater the savings. For couples with a large gap (e.g., $90,000 vs. $15,000), annual savings can reach **$4,000–$7,000/year**.

---

### Method 2 — CPP/QPP Pension Sharing

If one spouse receives significantly more CPP/QPP than the other, you can formally request pension sharing.

**How it works:**
The pension credits earned *during the years of shared life* are added together and divided equally between both spouses. It's not a "gift" — it's an official recalculation of amounts.

**Conditions (QPP — Retraite Québec):**
- Married, civil union, or common-law for at least 3 years
- Both are at least 60 years old
- At least one is already receiving their QPP pension

**Conditions (CPP — Service Canada):**
- Common-law or married partners for at least 1 year
- Both are at least 60 years old

**What you gain:**
- Reduced tax for the higher-income spouse
- Potential OAS protection if their income was approaching $95,323
- Annual savings potentially reaching **$3,000–$5,000** depending on the situation

**What you need to know:**
- This sharing is **permanent** (until separation/death)
- The shared amount depends on the years of shared life and contributions during that period
- It's not necessarily a 50-50 split

---

### Method 3 — Spousal RRSP (Do This Before Retirement)

If you're not yet retired, this strategy lets you "pre-position" future withdrawals at the lower-income spouse's tax rate.

The higher-earning spouse contributes to the partner's RRSP. At retirement, the holding spouse withdraws — paying tax at their more favourable rate.

**Important rule:** The holding spouse must wait 2 complete calendar years after the last contribution before withdrawing, otherwise the tax reverts to the contributor.

---

## 🛡️ Protecting OAS Through Income Sharing

OAS can be clawed back if your individual income exceeds **$95,323 in 2026**. Every dollar above this threshold costs you 15 cents of OAS.

Income splitting can therefore serve **two purposes simultaneously**: reduce taxes AND protect the higher-income spouse's OAS.

**Example:**
- Jacques: $100,000 in income → loses ($100,000 – $95,323) × 15% = **$701/year** in OAS
- Jacques splits $10,000 to spouse → income drops to $90,000
- **OAS clawback: $0** + additional tax savings on both returns

| Your Situation | Recommended Method |
|----------------|-------------------|
| You have RRIF income or an employer pension | T1032 pension splitting |
| Large gap in your CPP/QPP amounts | CPP/QPP pension sharing |
| Not yet retired, significant income gap | Spousal RRSP |
| Your income approaches $95,323 | Splitting to protect OAS |
| Similar income on both sides | Little or no splitting advantage |

## ❌ Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1: Never Filing Form T1032

Thousands of eligible couples have never completed this form. It's free, takes about 10 minutes, and can save thousands of dollars per year.

### Mistake 2: Attributing Too Much Income to the Lower-Income Spouse

If your spouse receives the Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS) or other income-tested benefits, giving them more income could reduce their government benefits. Run the numbers before deciding.

### Mistake 3: Thinking Splitting Is Permanent

Pension income splitting via T1032 must be renewed each year with your tax return. Skip a year and you lose that year's savings.

### Mistake 4: Ignoring the OAS Interaction

Always consider the combined effect: tax savings plus any OAS that's recovered. The real savings are often larger than they appear at first glance.

## ✅ Your Action Plan

### Before Your Next Tax Return
1. Calculate your individual taxable incomes for 2026 (you and your spouse)
2. Check whether you have eligible pension income for T1032
3. Estimate savings with and without splitting (CRA's free tax calculator or similar tools)

### This Year
1. Complete T1032 (and TP-1029.8.R-V in Quebec) with your upcoming returns
2. If CPP/QPP gap is significant: contact Service Canada / Retraite Québec about pension sharing
3. Check whether your individual income approaches $95,323 — splitting can protect you

### Every Fall (Before Year-End)
1. Reassess your strategy based on your year's actual income
2. Adjust your planned splitting amount accordingly
3. Consult a financial planner if your situation has changed (death, separation, income changes)

## ❓ Practical Questions

### "Is form T1032 complicated to complete?"

No. It's a one-page form with simple calculations. Most tax software (TurboTax, SimpleTax, etc.) includes this form and performs the calculations automatically.

### "Do we both need to sign?"

Yes — both spouses must sign and include form T1032 in their respective returns. The amounts must match between the two returns.

### "My spouse passed away this year. Can I still split?"

Pension splitting generally cannot be done in the year of death for pension income received after the date of death. Consult a tax specialist for rules specific to your situation.

### "We're separated but not yet divorced. Can we still split?"

In general, pension income splitting is only available to people who are living with their spouse on December 31 of the tax year. Check the rules applicable to your specific situation.
