# Contribution Limits 2025–2026: Everything That's Changing for Your Retirement Accounts

The rules for saving in your registered accounts keep evolving. For 2025 and 2026, the changes go well beyond just higher RRSP and TFSA limits — they touch CPP contributions, income tax brackets, Old Age Security clawbacks, and a brand new RRSP strategy worth knowing. Here's everything you need to plan smart.

---

## RRSP: Updated Limits and a Smarter Strategy

For **2025**, you can contribute up to **$32,490** to your RRSP. For **2026**, that ceiling rises to **$33,810**.

Your personal limit is the lesser of 18% of your previous year's earned income or the annual cap. For example, if you earned $100,000 in 2025, your 2026 RRSP room is $18,000 — well below the cap. You only reach the maximum if your income exceeds $188,000.

**Important for pension plan members:** If your employer contributes to a defined benefit pension on your behalf, a "pension adjustment" reduces your available RRSP room. This appears on your T4 each year and can significantly lower — or even eliminate — your personal RRSP contribution room.

### The "Fill the Bracket" Strategy

Instead of simply trying to maximize your RRSP, experienced planners use a targeted approach: contribute exactly enough to bring your taxable income down to the top of a lower federal bracket. This turns your RRSP into a precision tax tool, not just an account to top up blindly.

**How it works:**
1. Estimate your taxable income for the year
2. Look up your province's combined marginal tax rates
3. Calculate how much to contribute to land at the top of the lower bracket

*Example (Ontario):* If your income is around $155,000, contributing $5,000 to your RRSP drops you to $150,000, avoiding the 43.41% bracket on that $5,000 — a real saving of over $2,000 in taxes.

### The RRSP Season Deadline: March 3, 2026

You have until **March 3, 2026** to contribute to your RRSP and apply it against your **2025** taxes. Don't wait — financial institutions get extremely busy in the final days of RRSP season.

**Lesser-known tip:** You don't need to deposit fresh cash to make an RRSP contribution. You can transfer investments *in kind* from your TFSA or non-registered account into your RRSP — the transfer counts as a contribution. Moving from a TFSA to an RRSP triggers a TFSA withdrawal (you regain that room on January 1st of the following year) but no tax on the TFSA side. If you're in a 40% tax bracket, a $10,000 TFSA-to-RRSP transfer could generate a $4,000 tax refund.

## TFSA: Same Annual Limit, More Cumulative Room

The **2026 annual TFSA contribution limit stays at $7,000** — same as 2025. However, the cumulative lifetime limit for anyone who has been eligible since 2009 now reaches **$109,000** (up from $102,000 at the start of 2025).

**How withdrawals work:** Any amount you withdraw in 2025 is added back to your contribution room on **January 1, 2026** — along with the new $7,000 for the year. So if you withdrew $20,000 in 2025 and had $10,000 in unused room, you could deposit up to $37,000 starting January 1, 2026.

**One TFSA room rule to remember:** Your room is shared across *all* your TFSA accounts. If you hold TFSAs at two different institutions, the $7,000 annual limit applies to both combined — not $7,000 each.

### Front-Load Your TFSA on January 1st

Whenever possible, contribute your new $7,000 at the start of the year rather than spreading it over 12 months. The earlier your money is invested inside a TFSA, the more time it has to compound tax-free.

## 2026 Tax Changes That Affect Your Retirement Planning

### 1. First Federal Tax Bracket: Down from 15% to 14%

The federal rate on the lowest income bracket has been cut by 1 percentage point for 2026. For anyone earning more than $58,000, this represents a saving of approximately **$580 per year** — modest on its own, but meaningful when compounded over a decade of retirement.

### 2. Basic Personal Amount: $16,452

Every Canadian can earn up to $16,452 federally before owing any income tax. This amount rises with inflation each year and is especially relevant in early retirement if you're drawing down registered accounts gradually.

### 3. CPP Enhancement (CPP2) — New Contributions in 2026

Canada Pension Plan contributions have expanded significantly. Starting in 2026:

- **Base CPP:** 5.95% on earnings up to **$74,600** (up from $71,300 in 2025)
- **CPP2 (second layer):** 4% on earnings between **$74,600 and $85,000**

If you earn $85,000 or more, your total CPP employee contribution rises to approximately **$4,646** — up from about $3,356 in 2025. Self-employed Canadians pay both sides (employee + employer), roughly doubling that amount.

**What this means in retirement:** These additional contributions will eventually translate into higher CPP benefits. If you're already retired or within a few years of retirement, this change has minimal direct impact on your benefit — but it's good context if you have adult children still contributing.

### 4. OAS Clawback Threshold: Now $95,323

The Old Age Security recovery tax (clawback) now kicks in at **$95,323 of net income** — up from $93,454 in 2025. Above this threshold, OAS is reduced by 15 cents for every dollar of additional income.

This threshold matters for RRSP-to-RRIF conversion planning. Large RRIF withdrawals, capital gains, or one-time income spikes can push you into clawback territory. Strategic withdrawals — such as drawing down your RRSP gradually before age 71, or spreading income across years — can help you stay below this line.

### 5. Capital Gains: No Change (50% Inclusion Rate Stays)

In 2024, the federal government proposed raising the capital gains inclusion rate from 50% to 66.67% on gains above $250,000. This change was ultimately cancelled. The inclusion rate remains at **50%** for 2026 and beyond, which means investment planning based on the existing rules still holds.

### 6. Lifetime Capital Gains Exemption: $1,250,000

For Canadians who own qualifying small business shares or farm/fishing property, the confirmed lifetime capital gains exemption is **$1,250,000** for 2025. The 2026 limit has not yet been officially announced by the CRA — it will be published later in 2026 for the 2027 tax season. Consult a tax professional to confirm your remaining exemption if you've used part of it in prior years.

## First Home Savings Account (FHSA): Still One of the Best Tools

The FHSA remains unchanged for 2026:
- Annual contribution limit: **$8,000**
- Lifetime maximum: **$40,000**
- Maximum carryforward: **$8,000 per year**

The FHSA combines RRSP-style tax deductions on contributions with TFSA-style tax-free withdrawals when buying a qualifying home. If you're not yet certain whether you'll buy a home, opening an account now starts your contribution room clock — unused room carries forward, and if you ultimately don't buy, the funds can be rolled into your RRSP penalty-free.

**Home Buyers' Plan reminder:** The RRSP withdrawal limit for first-time home buyers was increased to **$60,000** (from $30,000). A couple who both qualify can now withdraw up to $120,000 combined from their RRSPs for a home purchase — a major planning opportunity.

## Account Priority: Which Accounts to Fill First in 2026?

As a general framework for Canadians building retirement savings:

1. **Employer matching (RRSP or pension):** Always capture the full match first — it's an instant 100% return
2. **FHSA** (if you qualify): Best of both worlds — RRSP deduction + tax-free withdrawal for a home
3. **TFSA:** Flexible, tax-free, ideal for lower-income years or funds you may need before retirement
4. **RRSP:** Most valuable when your current marginal rate exceeds your expected retirement rate — use the "fill the bracket" approach
5. **Non-registered accounts:** For overflow, prioritize tax-efficient investments (Canadian dividend stocks, growth ETFs)

## Pitfalls to Avoid

**Over-contributing to the TFSA:** Your room is shared across all accounts. Track withdrawals carefully — transferred amounts don't come back until January 1st of the following year.

**Over-contributing to the RRSP:** The 1% monthly penalty on excess amounts adds up fast. Check your Notice of Assessment or your CRA My Account to confirm your available room before contributing.

**Missing the RRSP deadline:** The deadline is firm. Contributions made after March 3, 2026, apply to the 2026 tax year, not 2025.

**Ignoring the pension adjustment:** If you belong to a workplace pension, your RRSP room may be much smaller than 18% of your income. Always verify.
