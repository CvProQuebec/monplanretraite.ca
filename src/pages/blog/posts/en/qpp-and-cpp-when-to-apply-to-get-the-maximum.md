# QPP and CPP: When to Apply to Get the Maximum

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 11 minutes*

**When you apply for your QPP or CPP, you're making a permanent decision that can be worth $50,000 to $100,000 over your lifetime. This guide gives you the exact calculations, the real break-even points, and the often-overlooked strategy of coordinating with your RRSP/RRIF.**

---

## 🏛️ QPP vs CPP: One Pension, Two Names

- **QPP (Quebec Pension Plan):** if you worked in Quebec
- **CPP (Canada Pension Plan):** if you worked in another province

If you worked in both, you receive a combined pension.

**2026 Reference Amounts (at age 65):**

| Plan | Maximum Monthly at Age 65 |
|------|--------------------------|
| QPP (Quebec) | ~$1,433 |
| CPP (Canada) | ~$1,395 |

> These amounts represent the maximum for someone who contributed at the maximum rate for ~39 years. Most people receive 60–80% of the maximum depending on their contribution history.

**New: The QPP/CPP2 Enhancement**
Since 2019, both plans have been enhanced to replace **33.33%** of your earnings (vs. 25% previously). If you contributed between 2019 and 2023, you have accumulated a second tranche (QPP2/CPP2) that adds to your base pension.

## ⏰ The Golden Rule: Timing Changes Everything

### Your Options by Age

| Age | Adjustment | % of Full Amount | Example on base $1,433/month |
|-----|-----------|-----------------|------------------------------|
| **60** | −0.6%/month | **64%** | $917/month |
| **62** | −0.6%/month | **78.4%** | $1,123/month |
| **65** | Reference | **100%** | $1,433/month |
| **67** | +0.7%/month | **116.8%** | $1,674/month |
| **70** | +0.7%/month | **142%** | $2,035/month |
| **72 (QPP only)** | Maximum | **158.8%** | $2,276/month |

> **Quebec particularity:** QPP increases up to age 72. CPP stops at 70.

### Simple Formula

- **Before 65:** you lose 0.6% × number of months early
- **After 65:** you gain 0.7% × number of months delayed

## 📊 The Calculation That Really Matters: Break-Even Points

Many people only look at monthly amounts. What really matters is the **break-even point** — the age at which waiting becomes the winning choice.

### Break-Even: Age 60 vs Age 65

- Money foregone by waiting 5 years: $917 × 60 months = **$55,020**
- Additional monthly gain: $1,433 − $917 = $516/month
- Time to recover: $55,020 ÷ $516 = 107 months = **~9 years after 65**
- **Break-even point: age 74** — if you live beyond 74, waiting until 65 is more profitable

### Break-Even: Age 65 vs Age 70

- Money foregone by waiting 5 years: $1,433 × 60 months = **$85,980**
- Monthly gain: $2,035 − $1,433 = $602/month
- Time to recover: $85,980 ÷ $602 = 143 months = **~12 years after 70**
- **Break-even point: age 82** — if you live beyond 82, waiting until 70 is more profitable

### In Perspective with Current Life Expectancy

A healthy 65-year-old Canadian has a life expectancy of **87 years (men) and 89 years (women)**. About half of healthy people will live beyond **90 years**.

**The actuaries' conclusion:** for a healthy person with other income to cover the waiting period, **deferring to age 70 is generally optimal**.

## 🎯 Strategy A: Take Early (Ages 60–64)

**✅ This strategy is appropriate if:**
- You have serious health problems or a reduced life expectancy
- You have no other income and need money now
- Your health no longer allows you to work
- You have less than $200,000 in savings

**Example — Marie, 60, physical worker with health problems:**
- At 60: $917 × 12 × 10 years = **$110,040** (if she lives to 70)
- If she waited until 65: $1,433 × 12 × 5 years = **$85,980**
- **Marie makes the right choice by taking at 60**

---

## 🎯 Strategy B: Wait (Ages 66–72)

**✅ This strategy is appropriate if:**
- You are in good health and your family is long-lived
- You have other income (RRSP, employer pension, investments)
- Your current income is high — adding CPP/QPP now would create a tax overload
- You want to maximize your protection against longevity risk

**Example — Robert, 65, retired engineer, excellent health:**

Option A: takes at 65 → $1,433 × 12 × 27 years (to 92) = **$464,292**
Option B: waits until 70 → $2,035 × 12 × 22 years = **$537,240**

**Robert gains $72,948 more by waiting** (not counting indexation, which widens the gap even further).

## 💡 The Often-Overlooked Strategy: Coordinating with the RRSP Meltdown

Here's what most guides on QPP/CPP don't explain: **deferring your pension to age 70 and using this time to execute the RRSP meltdown** is the most powerful combination for the majority of retirees.

### Why?

Between ages 60 and 70, while you wait for your pension, your tax brackets are relatively clear. This is the ideal time to gradually withdraw from your RRSP in low tax brackets.

At 72, when mandatory RRIF withdrawals kick in, your QPP/CPP will be at maximum — but your RRSP will also be smaller, so both together will stay in more favourable tax brackets.

### Example — Sylvia, 62, employer pension $42,000/year:

**Without RRSP meltdown + QPP taken at 62:**
- At 72: total income = $42,000 + RRIF $25,000 + QPP $1,123 × 12 + OAS $8,808 = **~$89,284**
- Dangerously approaching the OAS clawback threshold 2026: **$95,323**

**With RRSP meltdown + QPP deferred to 70:**
- Between 62 and 70: withdraw ~$12,000/year from RRSP (in the low bracket), RRSP reduced by ~$96,000
- At 72: RRIF reduced → smaller minimum withdrawal; QPP max = $2,035/month
- Total income at 72: $42,000 + RRIF $16,000 + QPP $24,420 + OAS $8,808 = **~$91,228**
- Full OAS preserved (below $95,323)
- **Total savings over 20 years: $60,000 to $120,000**

## 👶 OAS at 75: A Little-Known Bonus

At age 75, your OAS (Old Age Security) automatically increases by **10%** — no application required.

This permanent, indexed bonus means your liquidity needs at 75+ are partially covered automatically — another argument for not taking your QPP/CPP too early if you don't need it.

| Monthly OAS 2026 | Approximate Amount |
|------------------|-------------------|
| Ages 65–74 | ~$727/month |
| 75 and over | ~$800/month (+10%) |

## 👫 Strategies for Couples

### Smart Staggering

If one spouse is in worse health than the other, they can take their pension early while the other waits. You combine immediate security with long-term optimization.

**Example — The Michaud Couple:**
- Him (62, heart problems): takes his pension now
- Her (60, excellent health): waits until 67–68
- **Result:** security for him + optimization for her

### Survivor's Pension

If your spouse passes away, you are entitled to a **survivor's pension** in addition to your own pension. This amount is subject to a combined ceiling, but it's important protection — apply to Retraite Québec or Service Canada.

### CPP/QPP Pension Sharing

If one spouse has a significantly higher pension than the other, you can apply for pension sharing. See our article: [How to Share Retirement Income and Save on Taxes](/posts/en/income-sharing-couples)

## 🚨 The 5 Mistakes to Avoid

### Mistake 1: Taking Your Pension Early Just to "Not Leave Money on the Table"

If you have other income, this logic is misleading. Every dollar of CPP/QPP taken at 60 is a dollar that will never be enhanced. And if you live to 85+, you're leaving far more on the table.

### Mistake 2: Ignoring the Tax Impact

Your CPP/QPP is **fully taxable**. If you already have high income (employer pension, RRIF), adding CPP/QPP now can push you into a higher tax bracket — or trigger the OAS clawback (2026 threshold: **$95,323**).

### Mistake 3: Not Checking Your QPP2/CPP2 Enhancement

If you contributed after 2019, you're entitled to an additional enhanced pension. Check your statement on Mon dossier Retraite Québec or My Service Canada Account.

### Mistake 4: Deciding Alone Without Looking at the Couple's Situation

The optimal decision for a couple is rarely to take both pensions at the same age. Coordination is almost always more advantageous.

### Mistake 5: Forgetting to Apply

The government does **not** automatically send you your pension. You must apply approximately **6 months before** your desired start date.

## 📋 Quick Decision Guide 2026

| Situation | Recommendation |
|-----------|---------------|
| Poor health, need money | Take as early as 60 |
| Low income, no savings | Take at 65 |
| Employer pension + reasonable health | Wait 67–70 |
| High income, significant savings | Wait to 70 (RRSP meltdown) |
| Excellent health, long-lived family | Wait to maximum (70–72) |
| Total income approaching $95,323 | Deferring avoids OAS clawback |

## ✅ How to Apply

**QPP (Quebec):**
1. Check your statement at retraitequebec.gouv.qc.ca
2. Apply online or by phone: 1-800-463-5185
3. Apply **6 months in advance**

**CPP (other provinces):**
1. Check your statement on My Service Canada Account
2. Apply online or at 1-800-277-9914
3. Apply **6 months in advance**

**Required documents:** birth certificate, social insurance number, proof of address, banking information for direct deposit.
