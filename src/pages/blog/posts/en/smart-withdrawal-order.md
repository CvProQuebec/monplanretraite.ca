# The Smart Order for Withdrawing Your Money in Retirement

*Updated March 1, 2026 | Reading time: 14 minutes*

**After decades of saving, it's time to start drawing down. But watch out: a poor withdrawal order can cost you $50,000 to $200,000 in unnecessary taxes and lost government benefits. Here's the framework experts use to optimize every dollar.**

---

## 💡 **Why Withdrawal Order Is Worth a Fortune**

Most Canadians spend their entire working lives focused on accumulation. Few realize that the *decumulation* phase — how you withdraw, in what order, and when — can have an even greater impact on their final wealth.

> "Choosing the wrong account at the wrong time can cost more than a bad year in the markets."
> — Ferguson Financial Planning

Here's why order matters so much:
- Not all withdrawals are taxed the same way
- Some trigger OAS clawbacks (up to $1,500/year lost)
- Some don't appear on your tax return at all
- Any RRIF balance left at your death is taxed at up to 50% in your final year

**Concrete example**: Kathy, a retiree with $1M in her RRSP, compared two strategies:
- Strategy A (minimum withdrawals only): final estate of **$2.1 million**
- Strategy B (strategic RRSP meltdown): final estate of **$2.3 million**
- Difference: **+$200,000** from withdrawal sequencing alone

---

## 📊 **Understanding Your Retirement Income Sources**

Before deciding on order, map out your "retirement starting lineup":

| Source | Tax Treatment | Control |
|--------|--------------|---------|
| **CPP/QPP** | Taxable income | You choose timing (ages 60–70) |
| **Old Age Security (OAS)** | Taxable income | You choose timing (ages 65–70) |
| **RRIF** | Taxable income | Mandatory minimum from age 72 |
| **LIRA/LIF** | Taxable income | Mandatory min AND max withdrawals |
| **Non-registered accounts** | Capital gains (50% inclusion) | Flexible |
| **TFSA** | **Not taxable** | Completely flexible |
| **Defined benefit pension** | Taxable income | Per plan rules |
| **Annuity** | Taxable income | Fixed payments |

**Golden rule**: The TFSA is your most powerful control lever — its withdrawals don't appear in your taxable income, don't trigger any effect on government benefits, and don't reduce any tax credits.

## 🔄 **The Three Phases of Decumulation**

### **Phase 1: The Opportunity Window (Early Retirement, Ages 60–71)**

If you retire before age 71 — especially before receiving CPP and OAS — you have a golden window for the **RRSP meltdown** strategy (detailed below).

In this phase, your income is potentially at its lowest retirement level. This is the ideal time to:
- Draw down your RRSP at lower marginal rates
- Fill your tax brackets strategically
- Transfer surplus funds into your TFSA
- Defer CPP and OAS as long as possible

**Why defer CPP and OAS?**
- CPP/QPP: +0.7% per month deferred after age 65 → +42% if you wait until age 70
- OAS: +0.6% per month deferred → +36% if you wait until age 70
- Every month of deferral is a permanent, inflation-indexed increase

### **Phase 2: The Transition (Ages 65–71)**

CPP/QPP and/or OAS begin adding to your income. Key things to monitor:
- The impact of RRSP/RRIF withdrawals on the OAS clawback threshold ($95,323 in 2026)
- The pension income tax credit (first $2,000 of RRIF income qualifies)
- Pension income splitting with a spouse (up to 50%)

### **Phase 3: Mandatory Withdrawals (Age 72+)**

Starting at age 72, RRIF minimum withdrawals are mandatory and increase each year. The goals become:
- Using the TFSA to "smooth" income without triggering OAS clawbacks
- Managing the withdrawal order across sources to stay in the best bracket possible
- Planning for the tax impact of any remaining RRIF balance on the estate

## 🧮 **The "Bracket Filling" Strategy**

This is the expert approach: each year, plan your withdrawals to **strategically fill available tax brackets** — not too much, not too little.

### **Example: Michael, Age 68 (Ontario)**

Michael receives:
- Employer pension: $32,000/year
- CPP: $15,000/year
- Total fixed income: **$47,000/year**

His next tax bracket starts at approximately $57,375 (Ontario). He has about **$10,375 of room** in his current bracket.

**Bracket-filling strategy:**
1. Additional RRIF withdrawal: $10,000 → taxed within the same bracket → modest tax cost
2. For extra expenses (travel, renovations): TFSA withdrawal → **zero tax impact**
3. If needed urgently: non-registered investments → only 50% of capital gain is taxable

**Result**: Michael gradually depletes his RRIF at a moderate tax rate, while using the TFSA for discretionary spending without ever triggering OAS clawback.

## 🔥 **The RRSP Meltdown Strategy**

### **What It Is**

Instead of passively letting your RRSP grow until mandatory RRIF conversion at age 71, you make **planned, gradual withdrawals starting at age 60** — while your income is still relatively low and **before** CPP, OAS, and mandatory RRIF minimums stack on top.

The goal is to draw down the RRSP at lower tax rates and redeploy the after-tax proceeds into your TFSA (creating permanently tax-free assets) or non-registered accounts.

### **Case Study: Dave, Retirement at Age 60**

Dave has a $40,000/year employer pension and a $500,000 RRSP.

**Scenario 1 — No meltdown, CPP at age 60**
- Income: $40,000 pension + $8,448 CPP = $48,448
- RRSP withdrawal within same bracket: $44,000/year
- Total taxable: ~$93,000

**Scenario 2 — RRSP Meltdown + Defer CPP to Age 65**
- Income: $40,000 pension only (no CPP for 5 years)
- RRSP withdrawal within same bracket: **$53,000/year**
- Extra RRSP withdrawn over 5 years: **+$42,240** at no additional tax
- At age 65, CPP starts at ~$11,000/year (higher than at age 60)
- Benefit: $42,240 extra + permanently larger CPP

**Scenario 3 — RRSP Meltdown + Defer CPP to Age 70**
- 10-year window to withdraw RRSP at low rates
- Extra RRSP withdrawn over 10 years: **+$85,000** at no additional tax
- At age 70, CPP: ~$1,500/month (vs ~$700/month at age 60)
- Benefit: $85,000 extra + a significantly larger guaranteed pension for life

### **The Critical Rule: Meltdown and CPP Deferral Work Together**

The RRSP meltdown and CPP/OAS deferral are complementary — one creates the tax room that the other uses. Taking CPP early *fills up* the bracket room you were trying to use for RRSP withdrawals, eliminating the strategy's advantage.

### **When the RRSP Meltdown Is Less Effective**

- Modest RRSP balance (under ~$200,000): limited impact
- Late start (after age 70): window is too short
- Already high income: little bracket room to exploit
- Poor health or shortened life expectancy: the calculus changes

## 📉 **Avoiding the OAS Clawback**

The OAS clawback is a hidden tax that hits hard.

**2026 Clawback Threshold**: $95,323 net income
**Rate**: 15 cents lost for every dollar above the threshold
**Full clawback**: reached around $155,000 of income

What **counts** as income (triggers the clawback):
- RRIF withdrawals ✓
- CPP/QPP ✓
- OAS itself ✓
- Non-registered investment income (interest, dividends, 50% of capital gains) ✓

What does **NOT count** (no clawback impact):
- TFSA withdrawals ✗
- Return of capital ✗
- Life insurance proceeds ✗

**Strategy**: Use the TFSA as a buffer to stay under $95,323. Every dollar withdrawn from the TFSA instead of the RRIF means 15 cents of OAS clawback avoided.

### **Example: The Gagnon Couple (Age 70)**

Fixed income: $85,000 (CPP + OAS + pension)
Extra need for a trip: $15,000

- **RRIF option**: $85,000 + $15,000 = $100,000 → OAS clawback: ($100,000 – $95,323) × 0.15 = **$701/year** in lost OAS
- **TFSA option**: $85,000 (taxable) + $15,000 TFSA = **$85,000 taxable** → **$0 clawback**

Over 15 years of retirement, this adjustment can mean over **$10,000 in additional government benefits**.

## 🪣 **The Three-Bucket Strategy**

Many planners recommend thinking in three "buckets":

**Bucket 1 — Cash (0–2 years of expenses)**
- Contents: Chequing account, short-term GICs
- Purpose: Cover immediate expenses without selling assets at a loss

**Bucket 2 — Income (2–10 years)**
- Contents: Bonds, conservative ETFs, RRIF/annuity payments
- Purpose: Refill Bucket 1 gradually over time

**Bucket 3 — Growth (10+ years)**
- Contents: Equities, growth ETFs, TFSA
- Purpose: Beat inflation long-term; refill Bucket 2 over time

This approach helps psychologically — you never have to sell growth assets during a market downturn because your near-term needs are already covered.

## 💀 **The Estate Impact: The Hidden Cost of a Large RRIF at Death**

Here is a reality that few retirees anticipate: **any RRIF balance remaining at death is included in the deceased's final year income tax return.**

If you have $500,000 in your RRIF at death (with no surviving spouse), that amount is added to your final income. The marginal rate on this addition can reach **50%** (federal + provincial combined). Your heirs receive half.

**Comparison:**
- Remaining RRIF: up to 50% goes to taxes
- Remaining TFSA: 0% tax for heirs
- Non-registered accounts: only 50% of capital gain is taxable

**The takeaway**: The RRSP meltdown reduces the RRIF balance at death — one of the most significant estate planning benefits a financial planner can deliver for clients.

## 🚨 **6 Costly Mistakes to Avoid**

### **Mistake #1: Focusing Only on Account Balances**

"My RRIF is the biggest account, so I'll draw from that first."

**Problem**: This logic ignores tax rates, brackets, and OAS clawbacks.

### **Mistake #2: Treating the TFSA as an Emergency Fund Only**

The TFSA is your most powerful tax-smoothing tool. Using it only for emergencies leaves thousands of dollars on the table.

### **Mistake #3: Taking CPP Early While Doing the RRSP Meltdown**

These two strategies work against each other. Early CPP fills the bracket room you were trying to use for RRSP withdrawals at a low rate.

### **Mistake #4: Over-Melting Down**

Withdrawing more RRSP than your bracket can absorb is counterproductive — you pay more tax today than necessary. The goal is to *fill* the bracket, not exceed it.

### **Mistake #5: Taking Maximum LIF/LIRA Withdrawals Too Early**

LIF and LIRA accounts have mandatory minimums AND maximums. Taking the maximum early can push you past the OAS clawback threshold unnecessarily.

### **Mistake #6: Not Reviewing the Plan Each Year**

Tax thresholds, account balances, and market conditions change. A decumulation plan must be recalibrated annually — especially in October or November before year-end tax moves.

## ✅ **Your Decision Framework — Practical Summary**

### **If you're in early retirement (ages 60–71, before CPP/OAS)**

1. **Calculate your current fixed income** (pensions, investment income)
2. **Identify your bracket room** to the next tax threshold
3. **Make RRSP/RRIF withdrawals to fill that room** (RRSP meltdown)
4. **Transfer the surplus to your TFSA** if contribution room is available
5. **Use the TFSA for any additional expenses** without tax impact
6. **Defer CPP and OAS** as long as your situation allows

### **If you're in full retirement (age 72+, mandatory RRIF)**

1. **Take the mandatory RRIF minimum** (no choice)
2. **Calculate your total income**: RRIF + CPP + OAS + other fixed sources
3. **Compare with the OAS threshold** ($95,323 in 2026)
4. **If you need more**: use the TFSA first (zero tax impact)
5. **Then**: non-registered accounts (capital gains treatment)
6. **Split pension income** with your spouse if incomes are unequal

### **Account Withdrawal Comparison**

| Source | Taxable? | Counts Toward OAS Clawback? | Flexibility |
|--------|----------|-----------------------------|-------------|
| **RRIF** | ✅ Yes (full rate) | ✅ Yes | Mandatory minimum |
| **TFSA** | ❌ No | ❌ No | Full flexibility |
| **Non-registered** | ⚠️ Partial (cap gains 50%) | ✅ Partially | Good |
| **CPP/QPP** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes | Timing flexible |
| **OAS** | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes (itself) | Timing flexible |
