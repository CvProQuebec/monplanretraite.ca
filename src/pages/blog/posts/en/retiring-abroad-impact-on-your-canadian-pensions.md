# Retiring Abroad: Impact on Your Canadian Pensions

The high cost of living in Canada is pushing more and more retirees to consider life elsewhere: Portugal, Spain, Mexico, Thailand, Costa Rica. But before you pack your bags permanently, it's essential to understand how your departure will affect your government pensions, registered accounts, and tax situation.

The good news: your **QPP (Quebec Pension Plan)** and **CPP (Canada Pension Plan)** follow you anywhere in the world. But several other elements of your Canadian retirement become considerably more complex once you become a non-resident.

---

## ✅ What You Keep When You Move Abroad

### 1. Your QPP/CPP Pension

QPP and CPP are pension plans you contributed to throughout your career. Regardless of where you live in the world, you have the **right to these benefits** — they don't disappear if you leave Canada.

Payments can even be deposited directly into your bank account in your country of residence, in local currency. Retraite Québec (for QPP) and Service Canada (for CPP) allow these direct transfers.

### 2. Old Age Security (OAS) — With Conditions

OAS is a different benefit from QPP/CPP: it's a government program, not a contributory pension. It has **strict residency rules**:

- To receive OAS abroad, you must have resided in Canada for at least **20 years** after age 18
- If you have between 10 and 20 years of Canadian residence, you receive partial OAS **but only if you live in Canada**
- OAS is paid proportionally based on years of residence (40 years = 100%; 20 years = 50%, etc.)

**Example**: If you left Canada at 25, returned at 45, and retire at 65, you have 40 years of residence → you receive full OAS abroad.

### 3. Guaranteed Income Supplement (GIS)

GIS is **exclusively for Canadian residents**. If you leave Canada, you lose your GIS entitlement. It's a benefit for low-income seniors living in Canada.

## 🏦 Your Registered Accounts Abroad: RRSP, TFSA, LIRA

### RRSP: Maintained, but Taxed Differently

Your RRSP remains open after you leave. You continue to benefit from tax-deferred growth. However, when you make **withdrawals** from your RRSP from abroad:

- Canada withholds a **25% withholding tax** on each withdrawal (non-resident)
- If your country of residence has a tax treaty with Canada, this rate may be reduced to **15%**
- You cannot make new RRSP contributions once you're a non-resident

| Situation | Canadian Withholding on RRSP Withdrawals |
|---|---|
| Non-resident, country without treaty | 25% |
| Non-resident, country with treaty (e.g., France, Portugal) | 15% |
| Non-resident, Canada-US Tax Convention | 15-25% depending on type |

### TFSA: The Often-Ignored Trap

Your TFSA **stops growing tax-free** if you're a non-resident. Worse: if you contribute to a TFSA while non-resident, you're subject to a **1% per month penalty** on the contributed amounts.

Additionally, TFSA contribution room **does not accumulate** during your years of non-residency. You lose entire years of room.

**Advice**: Before leaving Canada, withdraw your TFSA funds (no additional tax for non-residents) or stop all contributions. Withdrawals don't generate extra tax, but contribution room only regenerates when you return to Canada.

### RRIF: Subject to Withholding Tax

Once converted to a RRIF, withdrawals are treated like RRSP withdrawals for non-residents: 25% withholding (or 15% with tax treaty). **Mandatory RRIF minimums** continue to apply even abroad.

## 💸 OAS Clawback Abroad

The OAS clawback still applies if you're a non-resident, based on your declared worldwide income. The 2026 threshold is **$95,323**: above this, OAS is recovered at 15 cents for each additional dollar.

However — and this is a lesser-known advantage — if you live in a country with a **tax treaty with Canada** that excludes the OAS clawback, you can receive full OAS **regardless of your worldwide income**.

> 💡 Examples of countries with tax treaties reducing or eliminating OAS clawback: Italy, Greece, New Zealand, certain provisions of the France-Canada convention.

## 🌍 Concrete Steps Before Leaving

### 1. Notify the Canadian Government

- Inform Service Canada of your change of address and residency status
- Complete form NR73 (Determination of Residency Status) to officially formalize your departure
- For OAS/QPP payments abroad, complete form ISP-3520 with Service Canada

### 2. Understand Your Destination Country

Each country has its own tax rules on foreign pension income. In some countries like **Portugal**, the "Non-Habitual Resident" (NHR) regime previously allowed a flat 10% tax on foreign pensions — a great deal for Canadian retirees (verify current rules as this regime has changed).

### 3. Check Whether a Tax Treaty Exists

Canada has concluded tax treaties with over 90 countries. These treaties determine:
- Which country has the right to tax your pension income
- The applicable withholding rate on RRSP/RRIF withdrawals
- Whether the OAS clawback applies

### 4. Form NR5 to Reduce Withholding

If your worldwide income is low, you can request a reduction in the Canadian withholding rate via form NR5. Once approved, this form is valid for 5 years.

### 5. Section 217 Election: For Low-Income Earners

The "Section 217 election" allows low-income non-residents to be taxed as if they were Canadian residents — which can significantly reduce their tax bill compared to the standard 25% withholding.

## ⚠️ 5 Mistakes to Avoid When Retiring Abroad

**1. Continuing to contribute to TFSA from abroad**
1% per month penalty on contributions — a very costly and frequent mistake.

**2. Not notifying Service Canada**
If you continue receiving OAS as a resident while you've actually left, you'll have to repay overpayments with interest.

**3. Forgetting the implications for provincial health insurance**
RAMQ (in Quebec) generally covers temporary absences up to 6 months. Beyond that, you lose coverage. Subscribe to international travel/health insurance before leaving permanently.

**4. Underestimating local taxes in your country of residence**
Your Canadian pensions could be taxed a second time in your host country, depending on local rules and the applicable tax treaty.

**5. Ignoring "de facto non-resident" status**
Even if you haven't officially changed your address, if you spend more than 183 days per year outside Canada for several years, the CRA may consider you a non-resident.

## 🗂️ Concrete Example: Louise and Pierre Move to Portugal

Louise (66) and Pierre (68) want to retire in Lisbon. They each have:
- QPP pension: $900/month
- OAS: ~$727/month (approximate 2026 rate)
- RRSP/RRIF: $350,000 and $250,000
- TFSA: $80,000 and $65,000

**Before leaving**:
- Withdraw all TFSA contents (no tax) and keep as cash or non-registered account
- Plan accelerated RRSP meltdown before departure to reduce future withdrawals subject to 15% withholding

**After leaving**:
- They receive QPP and OAS directly in Portugal (both have 20+ years of Canadian residence → full OAS abroad)
- RRIF withdrawals subject to 15% Canadian withholding (Canada-Portugal treaty)
- In Portugal, foreign pension income treatment depends on current rules — verify with a cross-border tax specialist

## ❓ FAQ — Retiring Abroad

**Q: Is my QPP pension reduced if I move abroad?**
No. Your QPP pension is calculated solely based on your years of contributions in Quebec. It doesn't change based on your current residency.

**Q: Can I open a new TFSA from abroad?**
No. You cannot contribute to a TFSA if you're not a Canadian resident. And new contributions made as a non-resident are subject to a severe penalty.

**Q: I'm returning to Canada after 5 years abroad. Do I get my TFSA room back?**
Yes. As soon as you return to resident status, you recover the contribution room accumulated before your departure, plus the room for the year of your return. Years spent abroad do not generate room.

**Q: My spouse stays in Canada. I move abroad. How does that work?**
Your spouse remains a Canadian resident and benefits from all normal rules. Your tax status is determined individually. In this case, the CRA will analyze your ties of residence (family, home, etc.) to determine if you're truly a non-resident.

**Q: Is GIS available abroad?**
No. GIS is exclusively available for Canadian residents. Upon your departure, you lose your GIS entitlement.

## 📋 Summary

Retiring abroad is entirely feasible financially, but requires careful preparation:

- **QPP/CPP** follows you anywhere in the world — no impact on the amount
- **OAS** requires 20 years of Canadian residence to be paid abroad
- **TFSA** must be withdrawn or frozen before departure — contributions abroad are penalized
- **RRSP/RRIF** withdrawals are subject to withholding tax (15-25% depending on the country)
- A **tax treaty** with your country of residence can significantly reduce your total tax burden
