# RRIF Decumulation Strategy: How to Avoid the RRSP Tax Bomb

*Updated March 16, 2026 | Reading time: 14 minutes*

**Your RRSP has been growing for 30 years - that is excellent news. But without a decumulation strategy, that same RRSP can become one of your biggest tax bills in retirement. Here is what experienced planners do, and what you can do yourself.**

You've spent decades building up your RRSP. You've done everything right â€” contributed consistently, let it grow tax-deferred, and now you're approaching retirement with a sizeable nest egg. But here's the problem no one tells you about: **your RRSP could be a ticking tax bomb**.

When you (or your surviving spouse) die with a large RRSP or RRIF, the entire balance is added to your income in the year of death. At a 50%+ marginal tax rate on large estates, the CRA could claim half your life savings. This guide shows you how to defuse that bomb â€” strategically, legally, and without leaving money on the table.

Your RRSP grows tax-deferred. That's the benefit. But the deferred taxes are a liability that follows you â€” and it compounds at the same rate as your portfolio.

The tax bomb is triggered in three scenarios:

1. **At death** â€” the full RRSP/RRIF balance becomes income in the year of death (unless transferred to a surviving spouse or dependent child)
2. **At RRIF conversion** (mandatory by December 31 of the year you turn 71) â€” you must start taking minimum withdrawals each year
3. **At large estate** â€” if your spouse also dies, the full amount hits the estate at top marginal rates

The solution is not to avoid the tax â€” it's to **spread it out over time** at lower marginal rates through smart decumulation.

The most important concept in RRSP decumulation is the **golden window** â€” the years between your retirement and age 71, during which:

- Your earned income has dropped (or stopped)
- CPP and OAS haven't started yet (if deferred)
- Your marginal tax rate is at its lowest

This window is your opportunity to **melt down your RRSP** â€” making strategic withdrawals at low tax rates â€” before you're forced into larger mandatory RRIF withdrawals and higher government income kicks in.

If you miss this window by doing nothing for 10 years, you'll face the scenario many retirees describe: RRIF minimums pushing your income into the 40-50% bracket, OAS clawback, and a massive tax bill for your estate.

## ðŸ”„ The RRSP Meltdown Strategy

The RRSP Meltdown (also called "RRSP decumulation" or "RRSP strip") is a deliberate strategy to empty your RRSP gradually during low-income years, paying tax at a low marginal rate rather than forcing a massive bill later.

### How it works:

1. **Retire early** (55-65) or reduce income significantly
2. **Delay CPP and OAS** until 70 (maximizing lifetime benefits)
3. **Withdraw from RRSP each year** during the low-income gap, targeting the lowest tax brackets
4. **Deposit excess into TFSA** â€” preserving capital tax-free for later
5. **Convert remaining RRSP to RRIF** at 71 with a much smaller balance

### Target annual income during the meltdown window

Most financial planners recommend targeting a taxable income that fills up the low marginal tax brackets:

| Province | Income Band | Approximate Marginal Rate |
|---|---|---|
| Quebec | $0 â€“ $51,780 | ~27-33% |
| Quebec | $51,780 â€“ $100,000 | ~38-45% |
| Other provinces | $0 â€“ $55,867 | ~20-26% (federal only + low provincial) |

The goal is to keep your annual income in the 27-38% range â€” well below the 50%+ rate your estate will face.

## â“ FAQ â€” RRIF & RRSP Decumulation

**Q: Can I transfer my RRIF back to an RRSP?**
No. Once converted to a RRIF, the account cannot be converted back to an RRSP.

**Q: Can I delay RRIF conversion past age 71?**
No. The law requires RRSP-to-RRIF conversion (or annuity purchase) by December 31 of the year you turn 71.

**Q: What if I need more income than the RRIF minimum?**
You can always withdraw more than the minimum. There's no maximum. Additional withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income.

**Q: Does the RRIF minimum change if markets drop significantly?**
No, the minimum is based on the January 1 account value each year. In 2020, the government temporarily reduced RRIF minimums by 25% due to COVID â€” this was a one-time exception.

**Q: What happens to my RRIF if I die?**
If you have a surviving spouse designated as successor annuitant, the RRIF transfers to them tax-free. If no spouse, the full value is included in your estate income for the year of death.
