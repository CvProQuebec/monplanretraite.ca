# The 5 Years Before Retirement: Your Ultimate Checklist

*Published March 1, 2026*

The 5 years before retirement are the most important of your entire working life. What you do, or fail to do, during this period will determine the quality of your retirement for the next 30 years.

Yet most Canadians reach this turning point without a plan. They have saved well, but have never thought about *how* to withdraw. That is a costly and avoidable mistake.

Here is your complete step-by-step guide to navigating these 5 years with confidence.

## Why the Last 5 Years Change Everything

For 30 or 40 years, you have been in **accumulation mode**: saving, investing, growing. But 5 years before retirement, you enter a **transition phase** that requires a completely different approach.

This shift catches most people off guard. Your next paycheck will stop. The questions become real: which account should you draw from first? When should you start CPP/QPP? How do you protect your portfolio if markets drop at the wrong time?

The good news: if you act now, you still have time to adjust course.

---

## 5 Years Before: Take Stock

### Calculate Your Retirement "Target Income"

The first question: how much do you need per month? Do not guess. Review your current expenses and identify what will disappear, such as work clothes, commuting, and savings contributions, and what will increase, such as travel, leisure, and healthcare.

The general rule says 70% of your current income. Reality: for many, 50% to 60% is more than enough. But *your* number depends on *your* desired lifestyle.

### Map Out Your Income Sources

List everything that will come in each month in retirement:

- **QPP/CPP (Quebec/Canada Pension Plan)**: Log in to your Retraite Quebec or Service Canada account for a personalized estimate. 2026 max: about **$1,433/month** at age 65 with maximum contributions.
- **Old Age Security (OAS)**: Available at 65. 2026 amounts: about **$727/month** (ages 65 to 74) and **$800/month** (ages 75 and up).
- **Employer pension plan**: Request projections from HR if applicable.
- **RRSP/RRIF**: Your accumulated savings, converted to a RRIF by age 71.
- **TFSA**: Tax-free withdrawals, used strategically.
- **Other investments**: Non-registered accounts, annuities, etc.

### Identify the Gap

Subtract your guaranteed income, QPP/CPP plus OAS, from your target income. The remainder must come from your personal savings. If the gap is large, you still have 5 years to act.

### Start Modeling Scenarios

Use tools like **SimulR** from Retraite Quebec to test different scenarios: retiring at 60 versus 65, delaying OAS, adding extra savings. The earlier you test, the more flexibility you have.

## 3 Years Before: The Plan Takes Shape

### The CPP/QPP Decision: When to Start?

This is one of the most important financial decisions of your life, yet most people make it without proper analysis.

**Taking it early (before 65)**: Reduction of 0.6% per month, up to 36% less at age 60.  
**Delaying (after 65)**: Increase of 0.7% per month, up to 42% more at age 70.

Rule of thumb: if you are in good health and do not need the income immediately, delaying often outperforms most investment strategies. The **break-even point** for delaying from 65 to 70 is typically around age 82 to 84.

> **Key strategy**: If your income between 60 and 70 is sufficient, because of RRSP withdrawals or a pension, consider delaying CPP/QPP until 70. You will receive 42% more for life, indexed to inflation.

### RRSP Drawdown: A Golden Window

Between ages 60 and 71, if your income is lower than it will be later, this is the **best time for gradual RRSP withdrawals**.

Why? At 71, you *must* convert to a RRIF and withdraw minimum amounts annually. These withdrawals stack with CPP/QPP and OAS and may push you above the OAS clawback threshold, **$95,323** in 2026.

Withdrawing earlier at lower tax rates reduces future tax pressure. This is known as an **RRSP meltdown strategy**.

### Eliminate Debt

Retirement with debt is stressful retirement. Focus your last 3 years on:

- Paying down your mortgage, or getting as close as possible
- Eliminating credit card balances
- Clearing car loans

A debt-free retiree needs far less monthly income to live comfortably.

### Plan Major Expenses *Before* Retirement

New car? Major renovations? Dream trip? Do them *while you still have employment income*. These costs are much easier to absorb before retirement than after.

## 1 Year Before: Final Adjustments

### Review Employer Benefits

Some benefits end the day you retire: drug insurance, life insurance, dental coverage. Identify them *now* and plan accordingly:

- Are you eligible for Quebec's public drug plan?
- Do you need private supplemental insurance?
- Do you have unused sick leave or vacation payouts?

### Write Your Withdrawal Plan

This is your financial roadmap. It answers: which account do you draw from first, and in what order?

A common strategy:

1. Draw from **non-registered accounts** first
2. Use **TFSA withdrawals** strategically when needed
3. Gradually withdraw from **RRSPs before 71**
4. Delay **CPP/QPP and OAS** if beneficial

The right order can save you **tens of thousands of dollars in tax** over retirement.

### Stress-Test Your Portfolio

Ask yourself:

- What if markets fall 20% the year I retire?
- What if I live to 95?
- What if inflation stays high?

If your plan survives these scenarios, you are on solid ground.

---

## The Mistakes to Avoid in the Final Stretch

Here are the most common mistakes:

- Waiting until the last minute to plan withdrawals
- Starting CPP/QPP at 60 without analysis
- Ignoring the OAS clawback
- Underestimating healthcare costs
- Retiring with too much debt
- Keeping an overly aggressive portfolio without a protection strategy

Avoiding just one of these mistakes can significantly improve your retirement.

---

## Final Checklist Before Retirement

Before you leave work, make sure you can answer "yes" to these questions:

- Do I know exactly how much I need each month?
- Have I listed all my retirement income sources?
- Do I know when to start CPP/QPP and OAS?
- Do I have a withdrawal plan?
- Have I reduced my debt?
- Have I planned for insurance and healthcare costs?
- Have I tested my plan against bad scenarios?

If yes, you are ready.

---

## Retirement Is Not Improvisation

The 5 years before retirement are not just another phase. They are the moment when your financial future becomes concrete.

The people who live the most peaceful retirements are not necessarily those who saved the most. They are the ones who planned the transition best.

If you use these 5 years wisely, you can enter retirement with clarity, confidence, and freedom.
