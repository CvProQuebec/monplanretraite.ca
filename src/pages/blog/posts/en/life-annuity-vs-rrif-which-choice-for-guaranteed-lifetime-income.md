# Life Annuity vs RRIF: Which Choice for Guaranteed Lifetime Income?

It's one of the most important decisions to make at the threshold of retirement: keep your savings in a **RRIF** (Registered Retirement Income Fund) and manage withdrawals yourself, or convert all or part to a **life annuity** with an insurance company for income guaranteed until death?

There is no universal answer. Both options have profound advantages and disadvantages, and the right solution depends on your health, risk tolerance, heirs, and need for stable income.

---

## 🔑 The Two Options at a Glance

### The RRIF (Registered Retirement Income Fund)

The RRIF is the natural conversion of your RRSP. You fund it by transferring your RRSP (mandatory before December 31 of the year you turn 71). Then, you must take a **minimum withdrawal** each year calculated based on your age, and you can take more if needed.

**Advantages**:
- Full control over your investments and withdrawals
- Flexibility to adjust withdrawals based on your needs
- Capital transferable to your spouse or heirs
- Benefits from financial market gains

**Risks**:
- **Longevity risk**: if you live very long, capital can be depleted
- **Market risk**: a bad sequence of returns early in retirement can be devastating
- **Behavioural risk**: in a panic, you may make poor decisions

### Life Annuity

A life annuity is a contract with an insurance company: you give them a lump sum of capital (often from your RRSP or non-registered account), and they pay you a fixed amount each month or year, for the rest of your life.

**Advantages**:
- **Guaranteed lifetime income** — even if you live to 100
- No market risk
- Complete simplicity: no investment decisions to make
- Protects against longevity risk

**Risks**:
- **Capital non-recoverable**: money given to the insurer generally cannot be recovered
- **Little flexibility**: no emergency withdrawal possible
- **Inflation risk**: a fixed annuity loses purchasing power over time (unless indexed)
- **No estate transfer** (unless with a guarantee period)

## 📊 Concrete Comparison: $200,000 in RRIF vs Annuity

Taking the example of a 65-year-old with $200,000 to invest for retirement:

| | Life Annuity | RRIF (GIC at 3%) | RRIF (Mixed Portfolio 5%) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Guaranteed annual income | ~$12,000/yr for life | ~$11,500/yr (not guaranteed) | ~$12,500/yr (estimated) |
| Residual capital at 85 | $0 (capital transferred) | ~$0 (depleted ~age 85 at 2%) | Variable with markets |
| Residual capital at 90 | $0 | Significant deficit | Variable |
| Guaranteed lifetime income | ✅ Yes | ❌ No | ❌ No |
| Flexibility | ❌ None | ✅ Full | ✅ Full |
| Transfer to heirs | ❌ No (unless guarantee period) | ✅ Yes | ✅ Yes |
| Inflation risk | ⚠️ High (if not indexed) | ⚠️ Moderate | ✅ Low with growth |

**Break-even point**: with an annuity paying $12,000/year for $200,000 invested, the "implicit return" equals what you'd recover in 17-18 years. If you live to 82-83, the annuity has paid as much as the RRIF. Beyond that, the annuity advantages you massively.

## ⚠️ The Central Issue: Longevity Risk

The core question here is simple: how long will you live?

According to Statistics Canada, a 65-year-old woman today has a life expectancy of **87 years**, and a man **84 years**. But life expectancy is an *average* — half of people will live beyond this age. And for a couple, the probability that at least one partner exceeds age 90 is approximately 50%.

**A life annuity specifically protects against this risk**: the longer you live, the more you benefit.

## 💡 The Hybrid Strategy: Best of Both Worlds

Rather than choosing between annuity or RRIF, most financial planners recommend a **hybrid approach**:

### Pillar 1 — Guaranteed Base Income (annuity + government pensions)

Cover 100% of your essential expenses (food, housing, healthcare) with guaranteed sources:
- CPP/QPP
- Old Age Security (OAS)
- Life annuity to fill the gap if pensions aren't enough

### Pillar 2 — Discretionary Income (RRIF + TFSA)

Keep a portion in RRIF or TFSA for:
- Variable expenses and leisure
- Emergencies and unexpected events
- A potential inheritance for your children

**Example**: You have $500,000 in your RRSP. You convert $150,000 to an annuity (guaranteeing ~$9,000/year for life) and keep $350,000 in a RRIF. Your government pensions + annuity cover essential needs, and the RRIF remains for flexibility and growth.

## 🏛️ The ALDA (Advanced Life Deferred Annuity)

Since 2020, Canadians can use the **ALDA** (Advanced Life Deferred Annuity): a deferred annuity purchased with registered money (RRSP/RRIF) whose payments begin at a chosen age, but no later than **age 85**.

**ALDA Advantages**:
- You pay less for an annuity whose payments start late (because remaining life expectancy is shorter)
- Excellent tool against "extreme longevity risk" (living to 90-95)
- You can allocate up to **25% of eligible registered accounts** (lifetime cap of $160,000 in 2026)
- Amounts allocated to an ALDA are exempt from RRIF minimum withdrawals until payments begin

---

## 🔄 Annuity With Guarantee Period

If the prospect of "losing your money" at death concerns you, most insurance companies offer options that partially protect your heirs:

- **10 or 15-year guarantee period**: if you die within 10 years, your heirs receive payments for the remainder of the guarantee period
- **Residual value**: option allowing heirs to recover a percentage of the initial capital if you die prematurely

The cost: these options slightly reduce the monthly guaranteed income amount.

---

## 💸 Tax Impact: Annuity vs RRIF

**RRIF**: all withdrawals are taxed as ordinary income. If you have a large RRIF ($600,000+), mandatory minimum withdrawals will increase as you age and can trigger OAS clawback if income exceeds **$95,323 in 2026**.

**Registered life annuity** (from an RRSP): payments are fully taxable as ordinary income — no particular tax advantage.

**Non-registered annuity** (from non-registered savings): only the "interest" portion is taxable, not the capital repayment. This can create a considerable tax advantage if you buy an annuity with non-registered savings.

> 💡 **Advanced Strategy: Insured Annuity**
> If you buy an annuity with non-registered savings, you can simultaneously take out life insurance so your heirs recover an amount equivalent to your initial capital. This maximizes annuity income while protecting the estate.

## ✅ Which Profile Should Favour an Annuity?

A life annuity is particularly appropriate if:
- ✅ You have **no heirs** to bequeath capital to
- ✅ You are in **good health** and anticipate living long
- ✅ You have little tolerance for market risk
- ✅ Your essential expenses **exceed** your government pensions
- ✅ You want **simplicity** — no longer managing your investments

A RRIF is preferable if:
- ✅ You have heirs to whom you want to leave an inheritance
- ✅ You have **compromised health** and a shorter life expectancy
- ✅ You want to keep **flexibility** for variable expenses
- ✅ Your base income (CPP/QPP + OAS) already covers your essential expenses

## ❓ FAQ — Annuity vs RRIF

**Q: Can I change my mind after buying an annuity?**
Generally, no. Once subscribed, an annuity contract is irrevocable. This is why you need to think carefully before this decision, ideally with a Certified Financial Planner.

**Q: What happens if the insurance company goes bankrupt?**
Assuris is the Canadian protection organization for life insurance policyholders. It protects up to 85% of guaranteed monthly benefits (minimum $2,000/month) in case of member insurer bankruptcy. The probability of a major Canadian life insurer going bankrupt is extremely low, but this protection exists.

**Q: Is an annuity indexed to inflation?**
Only if you choose an annuity with an indexation clause (which reduces the initial amount). Most retirees choose a fixed annuity due to the higher amount, but this means a progressive loss of purchasing power.

**Q: Can I buy an annuity with my TFSA?**
No. Annuities must be purchased with registered funds (RRSP/RRIF) or non-registered funds. The TFSA is not designed for purchasing life annuities.

## 📋 Summary

| Criterion | Life Annuity | RRIF |
|---|---|---|
| Guaranteed lifetime income | ✅ Yes | ❌ No |
| Capital flexibility and access | ❌ No | ✅ Yes |
| Longevity risk protection | ✅ Excellent | ❌ Limited |
| Inheritance for loved ones | ❌ No (without clause) | ✅ Yes |
| Management simplicity | ✅ Complete | ❌ Requires management |
| Market risk | ✅ None | ⚠️ Present |

**The best strategy for most retirees: combine both.** Cover essential expenses with guaranteed sources (pensions + annuity) and keep flexibility with your RRIF and TFSA.
